The aftershocks for the dollar

There remain many points of fragility in the US-Iran ceasefire, but markets appear to be settling down with a belief that the worst of the drawdowns are behind us. This gives us an opportunity to take stock on the dollar, how it behaved, and what may come.

This was not Liberation Day for the dollar. A simple analysis that looks at USD returns during drawdowns in a 60-40 US equity-bond portfolio suggests that the dollar behaved more "normally" in this conflict. The USD delivered positive returns as asset markets weakened. In other words, this was not Liberation Day when the USD fell alongside negative asset returns, shocking investors that may have expected the dollar to provide diversification for asset losses. Last year's price action had consequences, with growing discussion on raising dollar hedge ratios which meant that the dollar continued to weaken well after the trough in assets.

But the dollar's price action during the recent conflict is little source for relief. While correlations may have optically behaved better, we think more structural foundations of the dollar have been shaken in the past few weeks: namely the dollar's long-term role in global payments, savings, and trade.

In this note, we briefly share three points of focus for the dollar long-term:

1. The foundations of the petrodollar have come into question.

2. The worldï¿½s biggest savers may now need to use their own dollar-based savings.

3. Long-term trade flows in energy could shift to electro-states like China from dollar-based petro-states.

The aftershocks for the dollar may be felt for a long-time to come. We will be writing more on them.
