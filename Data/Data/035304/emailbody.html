DB thinks Fed policy is more accommodative than restrictive

US equities were a bit stronger Thursday, S&P up 0.6%, though European and Asian stocks lost a bit of ground: Stoxx600 down 0.1%, CSI300 down 0.6%. US10yr yields down 2bps to 4.28%. Oil up 1.5% to USD96.14/bbl (brent) though had been higher earlier in the US session as Israeli strikes on Lebanon continued and in the absence of a pickup in shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz. But oil prices fell back after the Israeli PM indicated that Israel would open direct negotiations with Lebanon, and after Trump asked Israel to reduce its attacks.

US core PCE deflator in line, printing at 0.4%mom in February and 3.0%yoy. Unrounded was 0.37%mom, and January was revised up from 0.36% to 0.39%mom. Core PCE now sitting at an annualized pace of 4.4%, 3.4% and 3.0% on a 3m, 6m and 12m basis, respectively. Market-based core PCE at 0.39%mom / 2.85%yoy. Personal income and spending both weaker than expected, especially income down 0.1%mom (consensus 0.3%mom); up spending 0.5%, consensus 0.6%mom.

Mixed US jobless claims. Initial claims up 16k to 219k, above consensus, continuing claims down 38k to 1794k, below consensus. Jump in initial claims likely a seasonal effect from Easter timing will be reversed.

Recall that at the March FOMC meeting, the median projection for the longer-run federal funds rate edged 10bps higher to 3.1%. Chair Powell described policy as ï¿½in that range where itï¿½s somewhere around the borderline between restrictive and not.ï¿½ But Matt Luzzetti argues that while Fed policy may be close to neutral, it is more likely to be accommodative than restrictive. The latest average reading from his r* dashboard is 1.6% (Q4-25). With US inflation well above 2% and projected to remain above target for some time, the current fed funds rate is likely below neutral. This conclusion is also consistent with the desire of ï¿½someï¿½ Fed officials, as revealed in the minutes, to adopt a more two-sided description of the policy outlook, one that includes the potential to raise rates if inflation stays elevated for longer.

Different investors continue to price in different probabilities for the potential outcomes from the Iran conflict, notes Luke Templeman. So no matter what next steps take place, there are some market dislocations that are still to be worked out of the system. The early setbacks during the ceasefire remind that a full de-escalation is still uncertain, but many global markets remain buoyed by the prospect of progress and the direct negotiations that will take place later this week.

In a similar vein, Mallika Sachdeva argues that markets appear to be settling down with a belief that the worst is behind us. This provides us an opportunity to take stock on the dollar, how it behaved, and what may come. And while correlations may have optically behaved better than during liberation day, structural foundations of the dollar have been shaken. (1) The foundations of the petrodollar have come into question; (2) the worldï¿½s biggest savers may now need to use their own dollar-based savings; and (3) long-term trade flows in energy could shift to electro-states like China from dollar-based petro-states.

Michel Kirker notes that the recent trajectory of energy prices presents a complex picture for the European Central Bank. Gas futures offer some reassurance by tracking the ECBï¿½s forecast baseline but brent oil futures are now roughly halfway to the ECB's 'adverse' scenario in 2026, and slightly higher than the adverse scenario further out. This points to a risk that the price shock could be more persistent than initially feared. DBï¿½s view remains that the risk of persistent inflation will likely drive the ECB to hike rates to 2.50% this year to signal its commitment to price stability, but the situation remains highly fluid and volatile.
