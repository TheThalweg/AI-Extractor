Australia

Labour force survey; RBAï¿½s Hauser speaks

The data highlight will be the labour force survey for March, due Thursday. We look for a 15k gain in employment and for the unemployment rate to hold steady at 4.3%. While employment beat expectations in February with a 48.9k rise, the details leaned weaker with a 0.2ppt jump in unemployment and the rise in employment concentrated in part-time jobs ï¿½ full-time employment fell ~30k.

Likely of more interest to market participants amid the ongoing Iran conflict, however, will be two speaking events by RBA Deputy Governor Hauser.

  • On Tuesday, at 8:15am Sydney time, Hauser is scheduled to deliver a fireside chat at an event at New York University.

  • On Thursday, 4pm Sydney time, Hauser will participate on a panel at the Institute of International Finance Global Outlook Forum in Washington.

Given that both these events are happening in front of US audiences rather than Australian ones, it is not clear whether there will be any messaging on the domestic policy outlook. Certainly, the RBAï¿½s recent rate path should be of interest for offshore market participants, but we will be watching to see if there is any message here as well for the local market. Recall that Hauser delivered hawkish remarks on a podcast one week prior to the RBAï¿½s March meeting, which were consistent with the 25bp rate hike that was subsequently delivered at that meeting (albeit with a 5-4 split vote). So if there is a fresh message from Hauser ahead of the upcoming meeting on 5 May, these speaking events provide a good opportunity to deliver it.

Elsewhere this week, Westpac consumer sentiment for April and the NAB business survey for March are due Tuesday, and will provide a sense of how businesses and households are seeing the impact of the Iran conflict. Consumer inflation expectations for April are due Thursday. Inflation expectations from this survey, as well as other measures, will take on more prominence at upcoming RBA board meetings as the RBA seeks to mitigate the impact of the supply-side shock to fuel prices for second-round price and wage setting. Little doubt that expectations will spike in this survey in light of the rise in petrol prices.
