dbDataInsights Gulf shipping tracker & global response - April 10th update
Our 'Gulf shipping tracker & global response' periodical focusses on tanker activity trends in the Middle East and energy-related newsflow during the ongoing Iran war. This report provides a daily update on the tanker movement data. In this edition we have moved to a slide deck format to provide better visualisations and include various commodity and economic pricing impacts.

* While President Trump has expressed optimism a peace deal with Iran will be reached, the Strait of Hormuz remains largely closed after a ï¿½fragileï¿½ US/Iran ceasefire was agreed on Tuesday. However, yesterday President Trump said Iran was doing a ï¿½very poor jobï¿½ of allowing oil through the Strait, claiming this ran contrary to what the two countries agreed which highlights both Iranï¿½s resolve to own the Strait and the complexities of the upcoming US-Iran negotiations scheduled for this weekend. The outcome of these talks will likely be key in determining future access through the Strait. 

* Tracking data point to minimal Strait of Hormuz crossings yesterday. Our preliminary data shows one confirmed tanker exit. This reflects tankers going West to East with their AIS transponder switched on. Media reports suggest up to seven vessels, including four tankers may have crossed yesterday, though the latter figure likely includes Iranï¿½s dark fleet movements. For clarity "vessels" include both tankers and other ship types (e.g., bulk carriers and container ships). While several tankers appear to be moving or waiting near the Strait, the bottom line is that traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains at less than 10% of pre-conflict levels with Iran continuing to restrict passage and shipping companies assessing risk (see slides 3 and 4).

* As a result, crude exports from Saudi Arabia have been moving through Yanbu port and have been averaging a robust 4.5 mb/d over the last one week. Further, we note that 5 VLCCs (2 mb cargo capacity each) have arrived at the Yanbu berths in the past two days, which will likely show up in stronger volumes in the next couple of days (see figure 5).

* Attacks on Saudi Arabian energy facilities have reportedly resulted in a 600k bpd cut to its oil production capacity, while throughput on the East-West Pipeline (key diversion as it avoids the Strait of Hormuz) has been reduced by 700k bpd. One such attack was confirmed on Wednesday, despite the ceasefire being in effect at the time. 

* The IRGC has released a map detailing its proposed safe passage route through the Strait, which appears to run closer to the Iranian coastline compared with the historical route. Iran claimed the new route was necessary to ensure avoidance of mines.  

* President Trump warned Iran against charging fees for passage through the Strait of Hormuz, as Iranian Leader Khamenei reiterated demands for a ï¿½new stageï¿½ of management of the Strait. UK PM Starmer and Italian PM Meloni also voiced opposition to a Hormuz toll, with reports claiming Iran was demanding up to $2mn in cryptocurrency to ensure safe passage. The burden of any toll charge would fall heavily on Gulf nations.

* Reuters reports President Trump has demanded firm commitments within days from European allies on measures to support the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. However, Chancellor Merz said Germany would not be able to meet such a timeframe, stating its involvement depended on an ï¿½international mandateï¿½ and a Bundestag resolution, alongside a peace deal.

* The EU Commission is set to propose ï¿½flexibilitiesï¿½ to methane emission rules set to apply to fossil fuel importers from January 2027, including less stringent rules for emissions tracking and the softening of potential non-compliance penalties.

Note: Our tanker activity analysis is based on tracking of Automatic Identification System (AIS) signals from ships. There have been reports of tankers exiting the Strait after switching off their AIS - we manually add to our data in case of confirmed third-party reports. Further, tankers exporting Iranian oil have typically kept their AIS switched off even prior to the conflict.
