News update ï¿½ North America, Europe & UK, Asia Pacific

 

North America 

  • The S&P 500 rose +0.6% to 6,824.66. The Dow Jones Industrial rose +0.6% to 48,185.80 and the Nasdaq Composite rose +0.8% to 22,822.42.
  • Cboe's Volatility Index fell to 19.49. The yield on 10-year Treasuries fell to 4.28%. The US Dollar Index rose to 98.92.
  • The US economy expanded at an annualized rate of 0.5% in Q4 2025, revised down further from 0.7% in the second estimate and 1.4% in the preliminary reading, mainly due to a downward revision to investment. Consumer spending slowed more than anticipated (1.9% vs 2% in the second estimate), as purchases of both goods (0.3%) and services (2.7%) cooled. Fixed investment also increased less than expected (1.5% vs 1.6%), largely due to drops in structures (-6.5%) while investment in equipment (4.3%) and intellectual property products (5.4%) remained robust.
  • Exports declined 3.2%, compared to a 3.3% fall, marking the largest contraction since Q2 2023. Imports also fell slightly less than early estimated (-1.0% vs. -1.1%). Meanwhile, government spending and investment contracted sharply (-5.6% vs -5.8%), subtracting 0.99 pp from overall growth, due to the government shutdown. For 2025, the US economy expanded by 2.1%.
  • Initial jobless claims in the US rose by 16,000 from the previous week to 219,000 on the period ending April 4th, ahead of market expectations of 212,000 to mark the largest initial claim count in one month. Still, the number remained firmly below the averages from the second half of the previous year.
  • Inflation-adjusted US personal spending inched up 0.1% month-over-month in February 2026, following no growth in January, signaling continued caution among households amid high inflation. Goods spending partially recovered, rising 0.2% after Januaryï¿½s 0.7% decline, driven by a 4.3% rebound in motor vehicle and parts purchases (vs. -4.2% in January).
  • US wholesale inventories rose by 0.8% month-over-month to $919.6 billion in February 2026, after a downwardly revised 0.3% decrease in January. Data came in stronger than the anticipated 0.4% fall. Nondurable goods stocks rose by 1%, rebounding from a 1.1% decrease in the prior month, as sharp rises in farm products (6.2%) and petroleum (5.2%) more than offset modest declines in miscellaneous nondurables (-0.3%) and groceries (-0.2%).
  • Amazon shares rose by +5.6% after CEO Andy Jassyï¿½s annual shareholder letter highlighted strong momentum in AI, specifically revealing that Amazon Web Services (AWS) AI services have reached a $15 billion annual revenue run rate.

 

Europe & UK

  • The STOXX Europe 600 fell -0.2% to 612.59, and UK's FTSE 100 fell -0.1% to 10,603.48. The Swiss Market Index rose +0.4% and France's CAC 40 fell -0.2%. Germany's DAX fell -1.1%, Spain's IBEX fell -0.2% and Italy's FTSE MIB rose +0.5%.
  • Germanyï¿½s 10-year Bund yield climbed back to 2.98% on Thursday, reversing a 14-basis-point drop from the previous session. Likewise, The yield on the UKï¿½s 10-year gilt increased by seven basis points to 4.72% on Thursday, rebounding from a 19-basis-point drop the previous day.
  • The surge in energy costs reignited fears of inflation, especially after Israelï¿½s deadliest airstrikes on Lebanon yet, killing hundreds and triggering Iranian threats of retaliation. Tehran dismissed further peace talks with the US, while the ongoing Strait of Hormuz blockade strained the already unstable truce. US President Donald Trump added to tensions by insisting US forces would stay near Iran until a ï¿½real agreementï¿½ is reached. Markets responded by pricing in tighter monetary policy, with traders now expecting at least two ECB rate hikes by the end of 2026.

 

Asia Pacific

  • Japan's Nikkei 225 lost -0.7% and South Korea's KOSPI fell -1.6%. The Shanghai Composite fell -0.7%. Singapore's STI fell -0.4%, India's BSE SENSEX fell -1.2%, and Australia's S&P/ASX 200 rose +0.2%.
  • Japanï¿½s consumer confidence index dropped to 33.3 in March 2026 from a downwardly revised 39.7 in the previous month, which was the highest figure since April 2019, below market forecasts of 38 and marking the lowest figure since May 2025. All components posted falls, including overall livelihood (29.7 vs 39.7 in February), income growth (39.8 vs 42.5), employment outlook (37.6 vs 44.0), and the willingness to purchase durable goods (26.0 vs 33.9).

 

Alternatives

  • Gold prices stabilized around $4,715 per ounce on Thursday as as investors weighed the fragility of the US-Iran ceasefire amid escalating Middle East conflict, while the surge in oil prices heightened concerns over energy inflation.
  •  WTI crude futures climbed nearly +5.0% to trade around $99 per barrel on Thursday, partially recovering from a -16.4% plunge in the previous session as renewed tensions in the Middle East raised doubts about whether a fragile ceasefire would hold. Prices moved higher after reports of increased military activity near the Strait of Hormuz heightened fears over potential disruptions to crude shipments, with sources indicating that some tanker traffic has faced delays.
