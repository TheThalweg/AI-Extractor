Brace for a housing market slump

We had high hopes for the housing market coming into 2026. After a tepid 2025, a more resounding housing market recovery looked likely. Interest rates were on their way down. Inflation was falling. Sentiment was improving. Geopolitical uncertainty was falling. And real disposable incomes were expected to remain firmly positive. Three months into 2026, however, the Iran conflict has likely put an end to any hopes of an imminent housing market recovery.

In this weekly, we reset our baseline expectations for house prices in the UK. We now expect a short slump in activity pulling down prices by 3-5% this year.

The Week Ahead: GDP, MPC speak, surveys

Things will start to heat up on the domestic front. We will hear from many MPC members in the week ahead. On the data docket, the key release to watch will be the February GDP report. We will also get the KPMG/REC Employment Survey on Monday, with the BRC sales data also out in the week ahead.

Key events in the week ahead
Deutsche Bank, Bloomberg Finance LP

GDP: After a disappointing January read, we remain upbeat about a Q1-26 jump in GDP. Indeed, survey data ahead of the Iran conflict have been generally positive. Our nowcast models point to growth of 0.2% m-o-m, led in part by stronger services activity (0.2% m-o-m), and a bounce-back in industrial production (0.6% m-o-m). We also expect construction output to have increased in February ï¿½ albeit marginally (0.3% m-o-m). Look for revisions too. Our models still point to a stronger January read, relative to preliminary outturn. Risks are balanced, in our view. And, while we remain optimistic about the February GDP print, we expect any positive momentum to come to an abrupt halt as the Middle East energy disruption lands on these shores. See our full nowcast note here.

Surveys: We will get two key surveys to watch in the week ahead. First, the KPMG/REC Employment Report. This will be a key report from recruiters gauging the state of play since the Iran conflict began. We're expecting gains in permanent job placements to reverse in March. Hiring demand we think will slow on the back of rising uncertainty and higher commodity costs. Vacancies, we expect, will drop further. And we expect pay awards to slow in March (at least on a seasonally- adjusted basis). What else to watch? We will be keeping a close eye on staff availability too. We think this can rise as the onset of a new cost of living wave kicks in. Second, keep an eye on the BRC sales data. We expect discretionary spending to drop in March as households brace for a new energy shock.

BoE Speak: On 14 April, we will hear from three MPC speakers. Chief among them will be Governor Bailey who is due to speak on a panel at Columbia University. A full speech will be published at 5pm UKT. We will also hear from external MPC members Catherine Mann and Megan Greene on the same day. Both sit in the more hawkish spectrum of the MPC, though Greene has also raised two-sided risks to the outlook. Elsewhere, we will hear from external MPC member Alan Taylor who will be making the rounds in Washington DC where he will be speaking at the Peterson Institute. Any commentary will be used to fine-tune market pricing heading into the April forecast round. What to watch for here? Has the recent ceasefire shifted the MPC's assessment of the policy outlook in any meaningful way? We maintain our call for no rate hikes this year, but see risks skewed in this direction.
