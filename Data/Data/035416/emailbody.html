US Economics Analyst: A Q&A on Our Economic Forecasts Amid the Evolving War with Iran
12 April 2026 | 10:14PM CDT | Research | Economics

嚙踝蕭

Following the announcement of a two-week ceasefire between the US and Iran last week, our oil strategists have largely maintained their baseline forecasts, which had assumed a six-week disruption of oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz. But oil flows remained depressed last week despite the ceasefire, negotiations between the US and Iran over the weekend did not produce an agreement, and President Trump has announced a US blockade of the Strait. As a result, we continue to see the risks as tilted toward a higher path for oil prices.

嚙踝蕭

This week we take stock of where our economic forecasts stand and what we嚙踝蕭ve learned so far.

嚙踝蕭

We have raised our December 2026 year-over-year headline PCE inflation forecast by 1pp to 3.1% and our core PCE inflation forecast by 0.3pp to 2.5% since the war began. Our analysis suggests that the impact of the war on core inflation should come mostly from energy cost passthrough, which is likely to have a large and fairly immediate effect on airfares but a more gradual impact elsewhere. Additional passthrough to consumer prices from the impact of non-energy Gulf commodity exports, including via higher food prices, should make a further modest contribution. Supply chain disruptions and shortages would be an additional upside risk if trade is disrupted for longer, but so far reported delays are minor compared to pandemic disruptions.

嚙踝蕭

We have lowered our 2025 Q4/Q4 GDP growth forecast by 0.5pp to 2.0% (or 2.3% on a full-year basis) since the war began. The downgrade mainly reflects the hit from higher oil prices to real income and consumer spending. Alternative data already show a large increase in spending on gasoline, which is likely to weigh on spending on cars and discretionary services. The additional drag from tighter financial conditions now looks more modest嚙碼our FCI had tightened by 75bp at its peak but has now tightened by 25bp on net, closer to the limited moves in prior recent wars. We do not expect much of an offsetting boost to GDP growth from increased investment by domestic energy producers, who are deterred by the volatility and uncertainty of oil prices, the market嚙踝蕭s expectation that prices will fall, and the 6-12 month lag before production begins.

嚙踝蕭

Weaker activity growth is likely to translate to weaker hiring and a higher unemployment rate, which we now expect to reach 4.6% later this year, versus our pre-war forecast of stabilization. Past oil shocks have tended to reduce employment the most in discretionary spending sectors like leisure and hospitality, where demand is most sensitive to the hit to disposable income.

嚙踝蕭

The spike in oil prices, increased uncertainty about the outlook, and the strong February employment report have kept the Fed firmly in wait-and-see mode for now. We expect a combination of rising unemployment and limited progress on inflation嚙碼where tariff effects dropping out should outweigh incoming energy passthrough嚙碼will make the case for two cuts in September and December. Our probability-weighted Fed forecast is considerably more dovish than market pricing, in part because we think rate hikes are quite unlikely in an environment where an oil price spike is much less likely to spark sustained high inflation than in 2022.
