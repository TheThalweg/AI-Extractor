The trajectory of energy prices resulting from the Iran war presents a complex picture for the European Central Bank. Recent rhetoric from Governing Council members suggests the economy is drifting towards the 'adverse' scenario outlined in the March staff projections. Our Chart of the Week shows that while gas futures offer some reassurance by tracking the baseline (Figure 2), the outlook for oil could be of more concern. Brent oil futures are now roughly halfway to the ECB's 'adverse' scenario in 2026, and slightly higher than the adverse scenario further out (Figure 4). That oil profile points to a risk that the price shock could be more persistent than initially feared.

The path of energy prices is critical because President Lagarde has affirmed the ECB will respond with an appropriately "forceful or persistent" tightening of monetary policy if energy price shocks risk becoming embedded in the wider economy. In her recent speech, she outlined a clear framework: the ECB will assess both the size and persistence of any shock as part of their assessment to gauge the risk of "indirect" and "second-round effects". Our own view is that the risk of persistent inflation will likely drive the ECB to hike rates to 2.50% this year to signal its commitment to price stability. Tracking where energy futures lie against the ECB's scenarios is therefore crucial, as it provides a clear signal for potential policy action.

However, the situation remains highly fluid and volatile. A recently announced two-week cease-fire briefly sent oil futures back towards the baseline, but the market's relief was short-lived as doubts about the deal's durability quickly pushed prices back up.

An updated assessment of the cost of energy imports: A few weeks ago, our Europe Chart of the Week assessed the impact on the energy import bill for the euro area (and individual member states) assuming oil and gas prices remained around their current levels. Based on the latest pricing (Brent at 98 USD/bbl, and gas at 47 EUR/MWh), the import cost has softened slightly to 0.8% of GDP (previously estimated at 0.9% when oil was USD105 and gas EUR55).
