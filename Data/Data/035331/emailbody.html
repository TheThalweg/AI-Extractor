SPECIAL EDITION ï¿½ U.S. and Iran Reach Fragile Truce: Now What?
(April 9, 2026 - Current as of 6PM EDT)
 

 

 

It took a little longer than we initially thought, but this chapter of the conflict with Iran ended as we expectedï¿½with both sides claiming victory, an uneasy ceasefire that leaves Iran bruised yet defiant, the core issues unresolved, and persistent uncertainty about whatï¿½s next. Given this, we expect Iranï¿½s future will preoccupy governments and markets for months to come.

Below we take stock of where things stand, where events are headed, and how this looks from Beijing to Brussels.

Key Calls

ï¿½     This ceasefire is a pause, not a peace. The two-week window wonï¿½t close the U.S.ï¿½Iran strategic gulfï¿½especially on the nuclear fileï¿½and the focus right now concentrates on one chokepoint: the Strait of Hormuz.

ï¿½     The global economy will continue to feel the impact from this war for months, even in the optimistic scenario where the ceasefire holds and transit through the Strait resumes soon.

ï¿½     Tactical wins arenï¿½t strategic victory. The U.S. and Israel hit hard and degraded Iranï¿½s military, but for now the regime is still standingï¿½intact, operating, and defiant.

ï¿½     A ï¿½Fortress Iranï¿½ remains most likely near term, but the post-conflict trajectory remains wide openï¿½four plausible paths with outsized implications for regional security and the global economy.


On Day 39, the U.S. and Iran agreed to a two-week ceasefire, which we could see coming. As we shared in our last note, the war was at a crossroads. The U.S. had a choice: find a diplomatic off-ramp or escalate with further military pressure, which raised risks significantly. As we anticipated, de-escalation prevailed.

ï¿½     This path was our base case because the incentives were clear. The 3Msï¿½munitions, markets, and midtermsï¿½have been shaping U.S. decision-making from the start. The pause conserves expensive U.S. firepower, while the prospect of an end to fighting is already lifting market sentiment.

ï¿½     Iran, too, had every reason to stop the bombing after more than a month of U.S. and Israeli strikes. Tehranï¿½s new military and civilian leaders could not consolidate power during active fighting.


Overall, the ceasefire is a positive and much-welcome development. It pulls both countries back from the brink, as tit-for-tat strikes on energy and civilian infrastructure could have devastated both Iran and the Gulf. There is now time and space for diplomacy. Markets are breathing a sigh of relief. 

But the ceasefire isnï¿½t the end of this story. Itï¿½s just the end of the beginning. As we have said for weeks, once the fighting stops, the struggle for Iranï¿½s future will continue.
 


The Test: Can the Ceasefire Hold?



The first step is in many ways the hardest: making the ceasefire stick. A two-week pause is not a settlement, and negotiations are scheduled for this weekend. A wide strategic gulf between the U.S. and Iran persistsï¿½so far neither side has backed down from its maximalist position on any issueï¿½and narrows to a chokepoint in the Strait of Hormuz.

  • Washington conditioned the ceasefire on ï¿½free and clearï¿½ transit in the Strait, while Tehran says passage is only safe if coordinated with its military. Freezing the war with Iran still acting as the de facto gatekeeper of the Strait would be a disastrous result for the U.S. and the Gulf countries.
  • For Iran, meanwhile, normalizing control of the Straitï¿½turning Hormuz into its own canal like Panama or Suezï¿½would be a strategic win. Yes, itï¿½s revenue. More importantly, it would cement the leverage Tehran built during this war. Tehran did not control the Strait before the war; it does now.  Thatï¿½s why Iran wonï¿½t give it up easilyï¿½and events have followed that logic. Rather than loosen control, Iran is doubling down, which may force Washington to make some uncomfortable concessions, perhaps on sanctions.


Relief Isnï¿½t a Reset


Even if this ceasefire endures and a deal to reopen Strait traffic materializesï¿½which is still highly uncertainï¿½it wonï¿½t flip a switch. The global economy doesnï¿½t just snap back to February 27.

  • In the best case scenario, our colleagues at JPMorgan Research expect it would take 2ï¿½3 weeks after a durable ceasefire before shippers are willing to run the route again and about two months to normalize port operations. All told, they estimate around four months to restore oil output and flowsï¿½assuming the Strait truly reopens. This means that energy prices would come down, but theyï¿½d still settle at a higher level than before.

ï¿½     Stretch the disruption and the damage compounds. If the Strait stays closed beyond six months, the risks shift from disruption to lasting damage: reservoir and field impairment that could lock in persistent supply losses.

ï¿½     The stakes go far beyond oil. Hormuz is also a chokepoint for fertilizer inputs like urea and sulfur, industrial helium (Qatar supplies one-third of global output, critical for semiconductors), and time-sensitive biopharma shipments.


Tactical Success, Strategic Ambiguity
 


Another reality check is warranted here: Hormuz may now be the center of gravity, but the core issues that pulled the U.S. to war in the first place remain largely unresolved.

ï¿½     Iranï¿½s conventional military capabilities and its ability to project power in the region have been severely degraded, yet it will take weeks to know exactly how much. Yet Iran does retain enough missile and drone capability to threaten its neighborhood. 

ï¿½     For now, the regime likely sees a core objective achieved: it is still standing after taking every U.S. and Israeli punch for nearly six weeks. And its enriched uranium stockpile and missile and drone capabilities survive, even if heavily batteredï¿½giving Tehran meaningful leverage at the negotiating table.

That is why what matters most is what comes next. With a fragile ceasefire in place, the fight for Iranï¿½s future is only just getting started.

Let's review the scenarios we laid out a few weeks ago...
 


What's Next In Iran?

 

 

 


While the regime held together through the bombingï¿½papering over rivalries to surviveï¿½the aftermath could be what breaks it. History suggests these post-conflict transitions unfold over time. Crucially, instability wonï¿½t end when the missiles stop flying: the post-war phase is where the internal reset begins. As the smoke clears, watch for competing elites and mounting internal fragility.

ï¿½     In the near term, our base case is what weï¿½re calling Fortress Iran: a weakened but durable regime whose security apparatus remains functional enough to enforce internal control, keep the state running, and still threaten the Strait of Hormuz. In this base case, Iran is unlikely to fully surrender its Hormuz leverage, while neither the U.S. nor Israel can fully disengage given the risk of Iran rebuilding its ability to threaten the region.

Over the next 1-3 years, however, our other scenarios remain in play. Fortress Iran could persist, especially if sanctions relief injects resources or the IRGC uses wartime success to justify tighter repression.

ï¿½     But again, durability isnï¿½t stability: as external pressure fades, elite rivalries can reigniteï¿½around succession and legitimacy, intra-security service tensions, and sidelined technocratsï¿½raising the odds of drift toward the other scenarios of a Weak Iran, Black Hole Iran, or a low-probability Normalizing Iran. We detailed these scenarios in our March 8 note.

Bottom line: itï¿½s far too early to call what kind of country emerges in the long term. The distribution is wide and the path is fragile, even if the longer-run direction is a weaker Iran with less capacity to export instability.

 

Whatï¿½s Next in the Region?


The regionï¿½s future canï¿½t be separated from what happens next in Iran. In the coming weeks, weï¿½ll be updating our assessment on how this war has further reshaped the Middle East chessboard. But for now, hereï¿½s what we do know:

Number 1: For Israel, the ceasefire represents a tactical resetï¿½not a strategic shift.

  • Israel will seek to ensure it keeps freedom of action against Hezbollah in Lebanon and in the Syria land bridge so that the pause doesnï¿½t provide cover for Iranian proxiesï¿½while giving the Israeli military time to rebuild interceptor stocks.
  • But Israel also finds itself at risk of being sidelined as a transactional U.S. Administration prioritizes global oil prices and economic stability over Israelï¿½s longstanding red lines.


Number 2: The Gulf countries are emerging from this war with a stark new reality, and their immediate priority will be convincing the U.S. that an Iran-controlled Strait of Hormuz is an unacceptable outcome.
 

  • If Iran keeps de facto control of Hormuz, Gulf states effectively lose maritime sovereigntyï¿½accelerating the push for export routes that bypass the Strait.
  • For the time being at least, Gulf states will be forced to co-exist with the same Iranian regime as beforeï¿½only now one that looks more entrenched and hardline. We could see a historic defense spending spree, with a focus on counter-drone and hardening key energy assets and data centers.


 

What Starts in the Middle East Doesnï¿½t Stay There


 

As we've highlighted before, the war with Iran is not just reshaping the regionï¿½itï¿½s reordering the global geopolitical landscape. This weekï¿½s ceasefire, after nearly six weeks of fighting, hasnï¿½t ended the story; it has made the shockwaves easier to see.  

It's tempting to sort players into "winners" and "losers," but the reality is messier. The war produced both tailwinds and headwindsï¿½just at different speeds.

Some headwinds hit now, others will lag. Some gains will likely prove temporary, while others will be more durable. Bottom line: todayï¿½s winners arenï¿½t necessarily tomorrowï¿½s.

Here's our take on which way the winds are blowing from the vantage points of Beijing, Moscow, Brussels, New Delhi, Tokyo, and Ankara.



China 

For China, the strategic tailwinds are looking favorable. The conflict has deepened allies and partnersï¿½ doubts about U.S. reliability. Many were already starting to ï¿½deriskï¿½ from the U.S. And with threats to abandon NATO and vital U.S. air defenses shifted from South Korea to the Middle East, Beijing expects U.S. partners to hedge further.  

ï¿½     To be clear, this conflict does not pull countries into Chinaï¿½s orbit. But it could weaken confidence in Taipei (and in other regional capitals) that the U.S. would have the will, staying power, and coalition support to defend Taiwan in a crisis. Beijingï¿½s military calculus may not have fundamentally changed, but President Xi now has a clearer picture of the political and operational environment heï¿½d be up against. 

ï¿½     Operationally, Beijing gained a real-time window into how the U.S. fights. The PLA can study burn rates, carrier movements, logistics, targeting, and escalation thresholdsï¿½while also observing Washingtonï¿½s reluctance to commit ground forces or absorb prolonged economic disruption.

ï¿½     Economically, the war will likely lead to a surge in investment in green technologyï¿½an area where China is already well-positioned. As Derek Chollet recently wrote in Semafor  with Dr. Sarah Kapnick, JPMorganï¿½s Global Head of Climate Advisory, this conflict reinforces the push to diversify energyï¿½including renewablesï¿½to reduce geopolitical chokepoint risk. China, dominant in solar and EVs, stands to benefit. Domestically, Chinaï¿½s coal base and strategic oil and gas reserves also help cushion the energy shock.

ï¿½     Diplomatically, reported Chinese pressure on Iran, outreach to Israel and Gulf capitals, and coordination with Pakistan also helps Beijing project itself as a pragmatic, constructive power.  

ï¿½     But the picture is not entirely positive for China. If inflation squeezes consumers and weakens demand for discretionary goodsï¿½including Chinese exportsï¿½that would hurt. And while the war has yielded valuable intel, it also underscored the unpredictability of asymmetric warfareï¿½and how much damage a weaker actor can still imposeï¿½an important cautionary lesson for PLA planners. 



Russia 

In the near term, Russia has hit a double jackpot: high oil prices and U.S. sanctions relief. Russian crude oil has surged from under $60 a barrel pre-war to above $110 ahead of the ceasefireï¿½more cash to bankroll its war in Ukraine.  

ï¿½     And itï¿½s not just pricesï¿½Russia is also gaining customers. Last month, the U.S. temporarily lifted sanctions on Russian oil already at sea, and buyers in Asia are lining up. The waiver expires on April 11, but an extension would be another boost. 

ï¿½     But Russiaï¿½s tailwinds are already fading. Ukraine is moving to blunt Russiaï¿½s windfall by escalating strikes on Russian oil terminals and other energy infrastructureï¿½damage that could be long term and expensive. Even if revenues hold, more cash doesnï¿½t automatically translate into battlefield success. Funding hasnï¿½t been Russiaï¿½s binding constraint.

ï¿½     Moscow may also have a Gulf problem brewing. President Zelenskyy signed air-defense deals with Gulf states last month, leveraging Ukraineï¿½s experience against Iranian-origin drones. Deeper Gulf-Ukraine ties could hurt Russia where it matters: the UAE has been a key financial hub for Russian firms and expats navigating sanctions since 2022.   



Europe 

At first blush, Europe looks like it comes out behind. Beyond the energy crunch and inflation risks starting to materialize, the biggest headwind is existential: the Iran conflict is further rattling the transatlantic alliance. The U.S.  was criticizing European allies as freeloaders long before this war, but Europe's reluctance to act in Iran has sharpened the Presidentï¿½s ire.  

ï¿½     We see U.S. threats to leave NATO primarily as a pressure tacticï¿½to rally support to reopen Hormuzï¿½and the U.S. remains legally bound by the treaty. But the damage is done: allies and adversaries alike are questioning Washingtonï¿½s commitment to collective defense (known as Article 5 in NATO parlance).  

ï¿½     Article 5 is NATOï¿½s core principle. The U.S. doesnï¿½t need to formally leave to weaken that deterrent: it can hollow out the alliance by raising doubts about whether it would show up, with rumors of U.S. force redeployments swirling. Trust once broken is not easily repaired. A rupture in the transatlantic alliance would be the single most consequential and lasting impact of this war, should it come to pass

ï¿½     But there is a partial offset. The war will likely accelerate Europeï¿½s push for ï¿½strategic autonomyï¿½ under a leakier U.S. security umbrella. UK Prime Minister Starmer has pointed to a ï¿½closer partnershipï¿½ with Europe, reinforcing the trend toward higher defense spending and deeper integrationï¿½early contours of a more Euro-centric security architecture.



India 

Indiaï¿½s default instinct is non-alignmentï¿½and this war has put that reflex under strain. New Delhi has functional ties with Tehran and a history of stop-start Iranian energy imports. But Indiaï¿½s stakes are bigger with the U.S. (trade, tech, defense) and increasingly with Israel on security, plus deep commercial and diaspora exposure across Gulf states lining up against Iran. So, India is tightrope-walkingï¿½pushing de-escalation while trying not to get boxed into a camp. 

ï¿½     This diplomatic dance is made harder still by Pakistan. Using its Gulf ties, China link, and closer ties to Washington, Pakistan is pitching itself as a Middle East mediatorï¿½hosting Iran talks, outflanking India diplomatically, and triggering alarm bells in New Delhi.

ï¿½     On U.S.ï¿½India ties, the conflict cuts both ways. In New Delhi, Washingtonï¿½s swings are seen as the new normal, reinforcing doubts about durable strategic alignment. Still, India will keep close economic and security tiesï¿½especially given Chinaï¿½and will want U.S. backing if the border heats up again.

ï¿½     The conflict also clouds Indiaï¿½s bet on Iranï¿½s Port of Chabaharï¿½a strategic-autonomy play to bypass Pakistan and counter Chinaï¿½s Gwadarï¿½now squeezed between renewed U.S. sanctions risk (India got a six-month waiver in October 2025) and an unstable Hormuz. If a longer-term U.S.ï¿½Iran deal brings sanctions relief, India would benefit as Chabahar can move forward.



Japan 

Japanï¿½s geography doesnï¿½t shield it from Middle East shocksï¿½and Tokyoï¿½s policy choices to date point to staying firmly aligned with the U.S., even though it resisted U.S. pressure to deploy military assets to reopen the Strait.  

ï¿½     The ceasefire has for now frozen the most controversial domestic debate: the legal threshold for exercising collective self-defenseï¿½staving off pre-ceasefire pressure from the U.S. to declare a ï¿½survival-threatening situation,ï¿½ which would have mandated a Japanese deployment to Hormuz. 

ï¿½     With Chinaï¿½s expanding influence in the Gulf and along key sea lanes, Tokyo sees its energy access at risk, particularly in the context of a period of weakness in China-Japan ties. Without the U.S. setting the terms of this war, Japanï¿½s Gulf oil and gas could hinge on an Iran aligned with Russia and China.  

ï¿½     At home, Japan is moving to diversify its energy mix, deepening energy cooperation with Indonesia to hedge against Hormuz-driven volatility. 



Turkey 

Turkey is also being squeezed economically and strategically. Turkish President Erdogan has closed its airspace to U.S. combat operations while condemning Iranian strikes against Gulf statesï¿½and ramping up border security to brace for a potential refugee wave. Ankara has spent the war balancing extreme energy vulnerability against the opportunity to act as a regional powerbroker.  

ï¿½     Economically, Turkey is taking a direct hit from higher energy prices and disrupted trade routes through Central Asia, intensifying already-elevated inflation pressures.  

ï¿½     Militarily, Ankara is using its mediator role to press for renewed access to U.S. weapons systemsï¿½highlighting that U.S.-made platforms have been central to intercepting Iranian missile attacks on NATO bases in Turkey.

ï¿½     Strategically, Ankara views an Iranian collapse as the worst-case outcome: a power vacuum that could strengthen Kurdish militants and destabilize the border. A refugee shock would add fuel to that fire. A pause in fighting, however, gives Turkey space to coordinate border security with Tehran and reduce near-term spillover risks.


This is just a sample of how countries have been impacted by the Iran War earthquake, and the geopolitical aftershocks wonï¿½t stop for many months.  

 


What Weï¿½re Watching

 

Uncertainty is high and headlines are noisy. Hereï¿½s what weï¿½re paying closest attention to:

ï¿½         Whether Islamabad talks produce progress toward a framework agreement, especially on uranium enrichment, despite the two sidesï¿½ demands remaining far apart;  

ï¿½         Whether Israel sustains its Lebanon offensive against Hezbollahï¿½which Tehran has demanded it stopï¿½and the extent to which that derails talks;

ï¿½         Whether ships start passing freely through the Strait of Hormuz or continue to stop and pay a fee to Iran;

ï¿½         Whether international partnersï¿½perhaps as part of the nascent UK-led coalitionï¿½deploy naval assets to the Strait to prevent Iran from keeping the waterway as its toll booth;

ï¿½         Whether the Houthis resume attacks on Israel to boost Iranï¿½s leverage and threaten another key Strait for oil flowsï¿½the Red Seaï¿½s Bab al-Mandeb;

ï¿½         Whether, now that the U.S. campaign has stopped, the new Supreme Leader shows proof of life (rumors abound that heï¿½s incapacitated)ï¿½and fissures start to emerge among elites.
