Compute bottlenecks now drive the thematic debateï¿½and the next leg of alpha will come from scarcity in power, labor, memory, and assets that AI cannot replicate.

  1. Since January, four linked forces have moved to the center of the market: non-linear LLM improvement, rapid compute demand growth, AI tech transfer, and AI-driven disruption, deflation, and labor pressure.
  2. The biggest story is still compute scarcity. Upcoming US LLM releases could widen that shortage and raise the value of bottlenecks across the AI stack, especially grid access, backup generation, memory, labor, and core data center components. Our updated Intelligence Factory model reflects the 55GW shortage in energy.
  3. We continue to favor the parts of the market that solve those bottlenecks. Powering AI and broader AI infrastructure remain central, but we also favor Defense, Healthcare, AI Adopters with pricing power, and Humanoids.
  4. We view our framework through the lens of thematic intersections. AI leadership, energy security, industrial policy, and labor disruption no longer sit in separate buckets; they reinforce each other and shape the next round of winners and laggards.
  5. From our 10 Thematic Predictions note, Prediction #1 remains intact: US frontier models appear set for a step-change in capabilities that Chinese peers may not match on the same timeline. That gap would intensify both adoption upside and disruption risk.
  6. Prediction #2 has strengthened. Token usage, agentic AI adoption, and model complexity all point to demand that outpaces available compute, which supports merchants of compute and companies that can add capacity fastest.
  7. Energy, as detailed in our March note, now sits at the core of the AI trade. Politics, affordability, and local backlash increasingly push data center developers toward off-grid and other ï¿½time to powerï¿½ solutions, while national energy security becomes a strategic issue in its own right.
  8. National self-sufficiency continues to rise in importance. Defense, critical minerals, manufacturing, and AI infrastructure all benefit as governments react to geopolitical stress, supply-chain risk, and uneven access to compute.
  9. Labor effects are no longer theoretical: we discuss this in our April AI Labor Disruption note. Survey data and company commentary suggest early AI-driven job displacement, rising reskilling demand, and growing policy sensitivity around employment disruption.
  10. Transformative AI continues to push investors toward assets with scarcity and staying power: physical infrastructure, proprietary data, network effects, regulatory permission, branded experiences, and other assets that intelligence alone cannot reproduce.
