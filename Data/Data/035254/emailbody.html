Core PCE inflation stays elevated

Core PCE inflation rose 0.37% m/m (3.0% y/y) in February, rounding to a 0.4% print for the second month running. Core goods prices accelerated from last month while services inflation moderated. We expect the core PCE-CPI wedge, which is unusually positive currently, to gradually close in 2026.

•

The run of elevated PCE inflation extended to February, with estimates about 4bp lower than our translation, but the January estimates were revised modestly higher. The 3mma for core PCE rose to 0.36% (from 0.20% in the prior three months ended in November), while the annual rate seems to be moving largely sideways since last year (Figure 1). Being a February report, it is somewhat dated, reflecting the BEA's lagging schedule following last year's government shutdown. This report carries no imprints of the US-Iran conflict. 

•

Core goods PCE accelerated to 0.8% m/m as we had forecast, and stands in contrast to the CPI measure, which showed just a 0.1% uptick in core goods prices for the same period. This is largely due to the differences in category weights, such that deflation in used cars matters less for PCE, while inflation in tariff​-​sensitive categories (apparel, household furnishings, other goods) is amplified. More interesting is how the uptick in computer software accessories is amplified substantially in the PCE price index due to weights, even though the underlying price data itself comes from the CPI. Indeed, ramifications of the global shortage in memory chips are seen more clearly in the PCE price index than in the CPI. 

•

We expect core PCE y/y to continue running higher than core CPI in H1, which we expect should keep the Fed on hold. The core PCE-CPI wedge, which has historically averaged negative 30​-​40bp, flipped positive in November and stands at +50bp in February. This unusual gap is due in part to category weight differences, "out​-​of​-​scope" PCE categories and the fact that the PCE index was not as severely distorted lower by the government shutdown as the CPI measure. However, we expect this gap to close fully by the end of 2026 as the CPI measure gets a mechanical upward boost in April 2026 from rents​/​OER and the PCE measure grinds lower in H2 2026 as tariff​-​related price pressures wane (see Unpacking the core PCE-CPI gap) (Figure 2). 

•

Our estimate of Q4/Q4 core PCE inflation in 2026 remains unchanged at 2.8% after today's data. We anticipate tariff​-​related price pressures to wane in the months ahead, which should help put a cap on core goods PCE inflation. On the other hand, health care services costs seem to have normalized towards their 12mma after a burst of inflation at the turn of the year, that was likely related to annual price resets. All told, we expect monthly core PCE inflation to return to target​-​consistent 0.2% m/m SA by H2 2026.
