FSA Fixed Income Update on Turkey Credit - Caught between oil shock relief and still fragile fundamentals

 

 

 

 

Kindly find below an update regarding Turkey Credit.

 

 

Background: the war mattered more for Turkey than for most of EEMEA

For Turkey, the US-Iran conflict was never mainly a ï¿½direct war exposureï¿½ story. It was primarily an oil, gas, inflation and external-balance story. Among EEMEA countries, Turkey screens as one of the most vulnerable to higher energy prices because it is a large net energy importer, and one of the countries with the biggest growth and current-account sensitivities to an oil shock.

 

That is why the ceasefire matters. The immediate positive for Turkey is straightforward: lower oil and gas prices ease pressure on inflation, the current account, FX reserves and domestic rates. A two-week ceasefire could trigger a sharp drop in oil prices, which should be seen as a near-term relief factor for Turkeyï¿½s sovereign, banks and energy-intensive corporates, even if the truce remains fragile and energy flows may not normalise immediately.

 

Sovereign: ceasefire helps, but does not remove the macro test

For sovereign credit, the key transmission channels remain imported energy, inflation and FX stability. Barclays estimates that, in a sustained $100/bbl scenario, Turkeyï¿½s CPI could take a direct hit of about 5.1pp and the current-account deficit could widen by roughly $25bn, or around 1.5% of GDP, putting more pressure on the FX framework and reserves.

 

The ceasefire therefore improves the direction of travel, but only partially. It reduces the immediate tail risk of a deeper energy shock and gives Ankara more room to continue its controlled-FX/disinflation approach. But the key message is that this is relief, not a full reset. Turkey still has to work through existing domestic constraints: real TRY appreciation is already weighing on parts of the export complex, inflation remains high, and a durable disinflation path still depends on tight financial conditions and continued confidence in the policy mix. The sovereign story is better if oil retraces and stays lower; it becomes difficult again if the ceasefire breaks down or if oil volatility re-intensifies.   

 

Central bank: already defensive, with more tools still available

The CBRT has already responded in a defensive way. They sold around $25bn of FX liquidity in the first three weeks of the shock, shifted effective funding to the 40% overnight lending rate from the 37% policy rate, resumed FX forward selling, and kept the lira on a predictable path. The CBRTï¿½s own March MPC summary says one-week repo auctions were suspended from March 2, average funding cost was kept at 40%, and TRY-settled FX forward selling was introduced to stabilise FX liquidity.     

 

For investors, the policy sequence still matters. If the ceasefire holds and oil eases further, the CBRT gets more flexibility to keep the current framework intact and eventually resume rate cuts later than previously hoped rather than abandon disinflation. If pressure returns, further options include tighter macroprudential measures, keeping effective funding above the policy rate for longer, tighter credit conditions and, if needed, more explicit rate hikes. Authorities could slow daily FX depreciation again once energy pressure fades, whereas under renewed stress they would likely first tighten via macroprudential and liquidity tools before allowing meaningfully faster TRY depreciation.   

 

Financials: still investable, but macro pressure argues for caution

Turkish banks are not direct losers from the conflict in the way industrial energy users are, but they are highly exposed to the macro consequences. The main risks are higher funding costs, delayed NIM recovery, weaker asset quality if tight conditions persist, and renewed sovereign/FX volatility. We therefore keep Turkey financials at Market-Perform view amid this backdrop and prefer the stronger-capitalised names. 

 

That cautious tone is also supported by rating commentary. Fitch said in mid-March that credit risks for the Turkish sovereign and banking sector from the Iran conflict depend heavily on the policy response, after Fitch had already revised Turkeyï¿½s sovereign outlook to Positive in January. Moodyï¿½s also upgraded Turkeyï¿½s sovereign ratings this year, showing that agencies still recognise the broader policy normalisation story, even as the war adds near-term macro risk. 

 

Overall banks are not the cleanest way to express a positive ceasefire view, but they are also not the weakest part of the Turkey credit story. They should stabilise if oil remains lower, but renewed volatility would likely hit the sector again through funding and sovereign-risk channels rather than through direct operating exposure.

 

Corporates: energy and freight are the pressure points

The main corporate shock channels are higher energy costs, higher freight and insurance costs, and, in selected cases, modest Middle East revenue exposure. Direct operating exposure to the region is generally limited for most Turkish issuers, so the bigger issue is cost inflation rather than lost revenues.   

 

The most exposed sectors are cyclical industrials. Cement, glass, soda ash, steel and white goods face the greatest pressure because they are energy-intensive and/or exposed to oil-linked raw materials and transport costs. We identify Arcelik, Limak Cement, Sisecam, and WE Soda among the more vulnerable names. We also note that Turkey relies heavily on imported gas, with Iran accounting for about 10% of supply, although LNG and other imports should mitigate outright shortages.   

 

That said, even in these weaker sectors, the message should not be overly bearish. There are offsets: energy pass-through is possible with a lag in several industries, BOTAï¿½ regulates domestic gas pricing rather than fully passing spot moves through immediately, and near-term maturity profiles are generally manageable. In other words, margins are pressured, but this is not automatically a refinancing crisis story.   

 

The more insulated pockets: telcos, staples and selected regulated names

The most insulated sectors are telecoms and, to a lesser extent, consumer staples. Telecom operators have low direct energy cost intensity and defensive demand. We explicitly sees Turkcell and Turk Telekom as having only limited energy-cost exposure, and prefers Turkcell for its stronger balance sheet and defensive profile.   

 

Consumer names such as Ulker, Efes and Coca-Cola Icecek look more mixed than outright weak. They still face transport and packaging cost pressure, but demand tends to be less cyclical and, in CCIï¿½s case, resin exposure is partly hedged. That makes them more resilient than heavy industry, even if not fully immune. 

 

Renewable power is one of the few relative beneficiaries because domestic power prices are correlated with gas costs, although the upside is capped by regulation. Energy distribution names may see short-term pressure, but typically recover this via tariff adjustments with a lag. TPAO is not a major windfall winner because domestic sales are regulated. 

 

Issuer-specific issues still matter more than geopolitics in some sectors

Within Turkish corporates, we remain less constructive on exporters. Real TRY appreciation is already weighing on competitiveness and margins, and the recent geopolitical shock has added pressure via energy and freight costs. This leaves export-oriented cyclical names less well placed than more defensive sectors such as telecoms and selected consumer names. That is why spread widening has been much larger in cyclical names than in defensive credits: Overall Turkey corporates underperformed, with particularly sharp widening in WE Soda and Limak, while telco spread widening was much more limited.

 

This is also visible in recent rating actions. S&P downgraded Arcelik in late March, while S&P affirmed TAV Airports and noted Middle East traffic exposure is relatively small at portfolio level. That supports a selective rather than broad-brush approach across Turkish corporates. 

 

Rating agencies: constructive on reform story, cautious on oil shock

Recent agency messaging is broadly consistent. Fitch revised Turkeyï¿½s sovereign outlook to Positive in January, Moodyï¿½s upgraded the sovereign this year, and Fitch also affirmed Turkiye Wealth Fund at BB- with a Positive Outlook following the sovereign action. At the same time, Fitch separately warned in March that the effect of the Iran war on Turkish banks depends on whether policymakers can contain the macro spillovers, and S&Pï¿½s recent scenario work highlights oil as a clear risk for Turkey among CEE/Turkey sovereigns. 

 

Bottom line: relief for credit, but selectivity still matters

The ceasefire is clearly credit-positive for Turkey because it lowers the probability of a more severe oil and gas shock. That is most supportive for the sovereign and for banks through lower macro stress, and for corporates through easing energy and freight pressure. But the country remains one of the more energy-sensitive stories in EEMEA, so this should be treated as a moderation of risk rather than a full all-clear.   

 

We prefer defensive and better-capitalised credits, remain cautious on energy-intensive cyclicals, and treat any ceasefire-driven tightening in weaker sectors as potentially fragile until lower oil prices prove durable and domestic disinflation gets back on track. Telecoms and selected staples look the cleanest relative shelters, banks are a cautious hold rather than an aggressive call, and heavy industry remains the area where issuer-specific margin and funding issues can still outweigh the geopolitical relief.   

 

 

Bond Shortlist: where we see relative resilience

For corporates: We prefer defensive, domestically resilient credits with low direct energy sensitivity and solid pricing power, while remaining cautious on export-oriented and cyclical names where real TRY appreciation, margin pressure, high funding costs and higher energy and freight expenses continue to weigh.

 

For banks: We prefer the stronger large private-sector banks with better capital, funding access and standalone resilience, while remaining more cautious on names that are more exposed to sovereign risk, tighter liquidity conditions and still-elevated domestic funding costs.
