Dollar barely decent. Markets continue to track the oil price closely. That's the case for the dollar (Figure 1), and also other key benchmarks like the US10y and S&P500. But the magnitude of the dollar's rise has been quite underwhelming - at the March peak it was up only 3% from end-February, and is now only ï¿½% above. That's not much for a liquid, safe, energy exporting currency. And it's not like a raft of safe alternatives did well - CHF and JPY are down over 2% since the war began. The dollar is bang in-line with a simple model that includes rates and oil (Figure 2). That's unusual - normally a premium gets built in during big global events (eg, the 2022 energy spike, Trump election). This is suggestive of a retreat from dollar assets, and is a bearish medium-term signal.

EUR & GBP trading well. That said, there could be a limit to near-term dollar downside on a trade-weighted basis assuming the Iran war settles further. Amongst the large currencies, EUR and GBP have done well (in the top-half of G10) despite heavy reliance on imported energy. That may reflect a hawkish rates repricing (especially for GBP) and a desire to park money away from the US. But further gains could get tougher. If the BoE actually delivers on pricing it could morph into a growth shock (especially given limited fiscal room), and weigh on GBP. And the eurozone growth story looks to have eased coming into March, with industrial production faltering. Relative data surprises are very low, which isn't helpful for EUR (Figure 3). It might be hard for these currencies to match the January highs in the near-term.

JPY still a waiting game. Could JPY start to perform? In trade-weighted terms it's around record lows (Figure 4), but policymaker concern has cooled if anything in the past week - perhaps as the key USD/JPY cross has done little. A decline in oil prices would help, but if there's a persistent premium built in vs pre-war levels, it could be enough for policymakers to stick to their dovish leanings. Japan's consumer confidence has taken a greater hit than elsewhere, which could encourage this stance (Figure 5). The market seems alive to this, given how little it has repriced the BoJ relative to peers. We continue to wait on a shift in thinking amongst policymakers and/or investors to get confidence in serious yen strength.

Look broadly. The set-up feels similar to earlier in the year, where none of the major currencies looked overly compelling, leaving our bearish dollar bias best expressed against other currencies. That includes EM, AUD, and NOK. On this score, it's notable that the recent COFER data shows an acceleration in appetite for smaller (unspecified) currencies amongst reserve managers (Figure 6). For the trade-weighted dollar to fall meaningfully, we need a shift in JPY and some better eurozone data.
