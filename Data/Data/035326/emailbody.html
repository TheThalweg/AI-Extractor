The Iran conflict has resulted in a classic negative oil supply shock. The impact, however, has had more wide-ranging consequences. While current levels are nowhere close to the lows seen during the aftermath of the pandemic, the latest PMI data showed that supply delivery times lengthened by the most since mid-2022. The recent Fed data also highlighted a similar trend with global supply chain pressures reaching their highest point since early 2023.

The latest BIC data from the ONS also highlights the growing concerns from business about supply chain disruption. In the UK's largest business survey, covering over ten thousand responses, firms highlighted a marked increase in supply chain concerns. In fact, over 50% of firms are now concerned about the impact of supply chain disruptions over the next year. As our chart of the week shows, the biggest driver for supply chain concerns came from 'international conflict' ï¿½ a clear nod to the ongoing Middle East disruption. The second biggest driver for increasing supply chain concerns came from 'shipping disruption' ï¿½ another negative byproduct of the energy shock.

Which industries saw the biggest concerns around supply chains? Transport and storage, for one. Leisure services also saw a big swing in supply chain disruption concerns. Professional services registered the third largest rise. The construction sector saw the fourth largest increase. And health and social services saw the fifth largest increase. Put simply, it's not just the manufacturing sector (which is energy heavy) that may be disrupted from the unfolding energy shock. Services sectors will also be impacted by any protracted disruption to supply chains adding to both activity and inflation concerns. We will be watching for direct and indirect effects as they unfold.
