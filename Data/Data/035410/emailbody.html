Recent performance trends

The sector, along with broader markets, staged a recovery this week due to developments in the ME and hopes that a ceasefire will hold. The risk on tone saw base and precious metals broadly rise from the start of the week, led by copper (+3%) and gold (+2%). As an energy input, thermal coal pared some of its gains with the active NEWC contract at ~$131/t following highs of ~$150/t (~$120/t before the conflict began). On a YTD basis, the sector is still in the green (SXPP +18%). Across our coverage, copper names are up +5-10% on the week, followed by RIO and BHP (+5-6%), and Hydro (+4%), while GLEN is slightly down due to its coal exposure. As we enter Q1 reporting (Fig 1-2), earnings upgrade risk (2026E EBITDA basis) for the majors sits at +5-10% and led by GLEN (Buy) due to robust thermal coal prices, although VALE (Hold) faces slight downgrade risk as iron prices have reached its lowest level in a month. Copper producersï¿½ upgrade risk is in the region of +10% with Hydro (Buy) at +5% with aluminium near ~$3,450/t. Refer to Fig 9-10 for our latest valuations.

Aluminium Expert Call and analysis

We hosted a well-attended Aluminium Expert Call on 1 April covering the crisis in the Middle East ('ME') and market implications (a replay is available on request). As detailed in our recent report (supply shock), the ME is an important global production hub, producing ~6.7 mt in 2025, making up ~23% of ex-China and ~9% of global supply. The two largest single-site smelters in the ME have been hit by Iranian strikes, Aluminium Bahrain's Alba (capacity 1.6 mtpa) and EGA's Al Taweelah (1.6 mtpa). Aluminium Bahrain has yet to provide an update, but the damage is believed to be extensive, while EGA recently confirmed its Al Taweelah site sustained significant damage and early indications are that a complete restoration of primary aluminium production could take up to 12 months. The Iran Aluminium Company (IRALCO) in Arak was struck by US and Israeli military operations earlier in the week (capacity 200 ktpa). The region is also heavily reliant on alumina imports through the Strait of Hormuz and two smelters (Qatalum and Alba) have already announced partial curtailments totalling almost ~600 ktpa due to energy and alumina shortages. The recent cuts to supply could put the market in a sizeable deficit in 2026/27, although demand risks remain elevated due to ongoing uncertainty surrounding the conflict. In our aluminium model, we assume a ~2 mt cut to supply from the ME, resulting in a slight decline in global primary output YoY in 2026.

Cobre Panama: a positive step towards reopening

FM (Hold) announced that it has received approval from the Government of Panama ('GoP') for the removal, processing, and export of stockpiled ore at Cobre Panama (CP), which could result in up to ~70 kt of copper production over a 12-month period. While this does not constitute a reopening of the mine, it is a positive development ahead of formal restart negotiations. We value FM's stake in CP at ~$18bn and estimate the share price is discounting a 10-15% haircut to this valuation; a restart without any changes to ownership or fiscal terms implies potential upside to ~C$41. Refer to our report for details.

Commodity price & FX trends (Fig 11-18)

Base metals moved broadly higher over the past week after a ceasefire in the ME was announced, with the moves higher led by copper (+3%) and zinc (+1%), while cobalt remained flat. Aluminium decreased (-2%) on eased supply disruption concerns. Precious metals also gained on de-escalation hopes; PGMs increased (+1-6%), followed by silver (+3%) and gold (+2%). Within bulks, NEWC (-5%) and coking coal (-2%) decreased, along with iron ore (-2%). Mining related currencies appreciated against the USD, led by the ZAR, the CLP, and the AUD (all +3%), followed by the BRL and the NOK (both +2%), while the KZT (-2%) depreciated.
