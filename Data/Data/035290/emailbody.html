USA: Q4 GDP Revised Down on Weaker Inventory Accumulation; Personal Income and Spending Below Expectations but Wholesale Inventories Stronger in February; Boosting Q1 GDP Tracking to 2.8%
9 April 2026 | 11:29AM EDT | Research | Economics

BOTTOM LINE: Real GDP growth was revised down 0.2pp to 0.5% annualized in 2025Q4, mostly reflecting a downward revision to inventory accumulation that should lower the bar for stronger GDP growth in 2026Q1. In addition, wholesale inventories rose 0.8% in February, well above consensus expectations. The core PCE price index rose 0.37% in February, in line with consensus expectations. Core goods prices rose 0.84%, the fastest pace since January 2022, likely reflecting continued tariff passthrough and a sharp increase in software prices. Initial jobless claims increased by somewhat more than expected to 219k in the week ended April 4th, while continuing claims declined to their lowest level since May 2024 in the week ended March 28th. We boosted our Q1 GDP tracking estimate by 0.1pp to +2.8% (quarter-over-quarter annualized), reflecting stronger-than-expected inventory accumulation partially offset by softer-than-expected consumption growth in January and February.

MAIN POINTS:

1. Real GDP growth was revised down 0.2pp to 0.5% annualized in 2025Q4, mostly reflecting a downward revision to inventory accumulation that should lower the bar for stronger GDP growth in 2026Q1. Real gross domestic income (GDI) rose 2.6% annualized in Q4, partially reflecting strong growth in corporate profits. Real private domestic final sales growth was revised down 0.1pp to 1.8%, reflecting downward revisions to consumer spending (-0.1pp to 1.9%) and housing investment (-1.1pp to -1.7%) growth.

2. The core PCE price index rose 0.37% in February, in line with consensus expectations, and the year-over-year rate edged down to 2.97%. Core goods prices rose 0.84% (the fastest pace since January 2022), likely reflecting continued tariff passthrough and a 6.5% month-over-month increase in software prices, the second-largest since 1980 (after a 7.0% increase in December 2025). Core services prices rose 0.22%. Headline PCE rose 0.38% in February, reflecting a 0.45% increase in food and energy prices.

3. Personal income declined 0.1% (or $18bn) in February, against expectations for an increase. Employee compensation rose $33bn, proprietorsï¿½ income rose $11bn, and interest income rose $5bn, while dividend income declined $40bn (reversing a similarly-sized increase in January) and transfer income declined $22bn. Personal spending rose 0.5% in February, and January spending growth was revised down 0.1pp to 0.3%. The saving rate declined to 4.0%, roughly in line with its 2025Q4 level.

4. Wholesale inventories increased by 0.8% in February, well above consensus expectations. Wholesale sales ex-petroleum increased 2.6% and the wholesale inventory-to-sales ratio decreased from 1.25 to 1.22, just above its trough of 1.20 in July 2021.

5. Initial jobless claims rose 16k to 219k in the week ended April 4th, somewhat above expectations. The four-week moving average of claims increased 2k to 210k. Claims increased by 4k in New Jersey and 2k in Pennsylvania and did not decline by 2k or more in any state (all state-level data seasonally adjusted by GS). Nationwide continuing claimsï¿½the number of persons receiving benefits through standard programsï¿½declined 38k to 1,794k in the week ended March 28th, somewhat below consensus expectations and the lowest level since May 2024.

6. The details of the personal consumption report were softer than our previous GDP tracking assumptions, while February growth in wholesale inventories was stronger. On net, we boosted our Q1 GDP tracking estimate by 0.1pp to +2.8% (quarter-over-quarter annualized).
