dbDataInsights Gulf shipping tracker & global response - March 30th
This first part of this report provides the latest data on tanker movements in the Strait of Hormuz and other key Gulf shipping routes. The second part summarises notable developments in the global response.

See figures 1-3 in this report for updated shipping data.

Key updates

  • As per our data (based on tracking of ship AIS signals or confirmed crossings), four tankers with a combined DWT of 329K exited the Strait of Hormuz between March 27-29th. The four vessels include two Indian LPG tankers and one crude oil tanker journeying to Pakistan.
  • The UK Maritime Trade Operations Center reported 11 vessels crossed the Strait last Saturday (unclear if any were tankers, though it is possible a few transited the SoH with their AIS turned off), while three Chinese container ships were unsuccessful in an attempted crossing on March 27.
  • Saudi Arabia's East-West pipeline is reportedly now operating at maximum capacity (7mn bpd), with crude exports from Yanbu at 5mn bpd and refined product exports at 700-900k bpd. However, Houthi Red Sea attacks are a key risk following recent missile launches at Israel.
  • President Trump reiterated his desire to wind down hostilities quickly, adding Iran had agreed to allow up to 20 oil tankers through the Strait as a "sign of respect". Trump also did not rule out seizing Kharg Island and pushed back on the prospect of a toll for ships crossing the SoH, despite continued discussion about Iran imposing a $2mn fee.
  • Pakistan said preparations continue for US-Iran ceasefire talks that may take place as early as this week.
  • Aluminium prices have risen following attacks on two Middle-Eastern producers. The region collectively accounts for 9% of global supply. Concerns are also being flagged over a helium shortage that may hurt the tech industry.
  • On the prospect of rate hikes, French Central Bank Governor Villeroy said "we are ready to act in this direction if needed", but added "the debate on pre-established dates appears very premature".
