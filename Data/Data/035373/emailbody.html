So far the market's attention has predominantly focused on the risk of persistent inflation stemming from the energy shock rather than on the potential for growth to be hit harder than assumed. This week's ceasefire announcement is positive news. But uncertainties persist, oil prices remain elevated and in this note we focus on the downside risks to growth.

In the first half of this note we look at growth conditions in the euro area in early 2026 prior to the start of the conflict in the Middle East. There is a growing risk that economic growth was softer at the start of this year than the consensus and ECB projections. In other words, there may have been less of a growth buffer to absorb an energy shock.

With oil prices remaining elevated despite the ceasefire announcement ï¿½ 2027 oil forwards are above the ECB's 'adverse' scenario ï¿½ in the second half of this note we explore the ECB's growth assumptions under the alternative scenarios. Some of the assumptions are optimistic, in our view. A more persistent energy shock could potentially hit growth harder than the ECB thinks. We show that by tweaking the assumptions on the labour market, uncertainty and savings behaviour, GDP growth in 2026 under the ECB's 'severe' scenario could fall to 0.1% from 0.4%.
