  • March's headline CPI (+0.87% vs +0.27% in February) was dominated by the expected 21% surge in energy commodity prices. However, core CPI (+0.20% vs. 0.22%) was a bit softer than we anticipated. The year-over-year rate for headline jumped from 2.4% to 3.3%, while that for core only ticked up from 2.5% to 2.6%.

  • Within the basket, core goods were softer than expected, with a 1% decline in medical care commodities and a 0.4% fall in used car and truck prices. The latter is unlikely to persist given recent increases in wholesale used car prices. Conversely, apparel prices showed a notable 1% increase, likely a function of lagged tariff effects.

  • On the core services side, rents showed a slight uptick but remained relatively moderate. Medical services were softer, while airfares increased by 2.7%. The latter is one area in which higher energy prices could bleed through into core inflation going forward.

  • In terms of the readthrough into core PCE, the Fedï¿½s preferred inflation metric, we are tracking a 0.22% March print, down from our 0.27% tracking prior to the CPI print. This would have the year-over-year rate tick back up a tenth to 3.1%. Next weekï¿½s PPI data will provide more precision around this estimate.

  • While the core print was weaker than expected, it does not materially change our view of the inflation narrative. Inflation in services excluding housing remains stubbornly elevated. At the same time, passthrough from tariffs and higher energy prices are clear upside risks going forward.
