U.S. Markets Recap

Dow +0.58%, US +0.62%, Nasdaq +0.83%

 

Synopsis

ï¿½                     US equities higher in Thursday trading, ending somewhat off best levels. S&P 500 logged its seventh straight gain; index now a bit more than 2% below its 27-Jan ATH. Big tech was mostly better with AMZN and META the notable gainers. Market helped by some easing of ceasefire durability concerns related to the Lebanon issue (Israel has agreed to direct talks). In addition, tensions in the region have simmered down on Thursday, US-Iran negotiations still set for Saturday morning in Pakistan, and broader offramp optimism remains intact. Positioning dynamics still cited as supportive. Goldman Sachs noted CTAs set to buy $34B of SPX over next week (flipping from short to long). Select Mag 7 strength another tailwind with AMZN a standout following CEO letter highlight leverage to compute demand and strength of chips business. Compute demand dynamic also supported by expanded deal between CRWV and META. However, AI disruption theme back as a big overhang on software with focus on powerful new LLMs (too powerful for broad release). Software woes also keeping the scrutiny on private credit (wall of software-related debt coming due, outsized redemption requests at CG flagship fund). Elsewhere, GOOGL and INTC announced expansion of AI/cloud partnership. OpenAI reportedly set to follow Anthropic in limiting release of new model due to cybersecurity concerns. DC appeals court denied Anthropic request to pause Pentagon supply chain risk designation. COST reported solid March sales. DIS reportedly planning to cut 1K jobs, the bulk of which will be in the recently consolidated marketing department. STZ fiscal Q4 better with analysts highlighting shift to positive depletions and strong category tailwinds ahead. APLD lagged despite its big fiscal Q3 beat and upbeat commentary on AI data center capacity demand, with FCF much worse than expected and no new customer announcement. SMPL missed on revenue and cut guidance. NEOG disappointed on Animal Safety and core growth. STAA FQ3 prelim revenue well ahead of consensus with strong growth in China and Americas.

ï¿½                     Sector Performance: Consumer Disc. +2.46%, Industrials +1.04%, Communication Svcs. +0.93%, Consumer Spls. +0.88%, Utilities +0.84%, Real Estate +0.83%, Tech +0.38%, Financials +0.27%, Materials +0.10%, Healthcare (0.19%), Energy (1.16%),

 

Macro

ï¿½                     Weekly initial jobless claims printed at 219K, above 210K consensus and prior week's upwardly revised 203K (was 202K). Continuing claims came in at 1794K against 1840K forecasts, below prior 1832K (revised down from 1841K); lowest since June 2024. February personal income down 0.1% m/m vs expectations for a 0.3% rise; personal spending up 0.5% m/m, in line with consensus. February core PCE at 0.4%, in line with forecasts and level with January. Final read of Q4 GDP down to a 0.5% SAAR vs 0.7% in the first revision; final Q4 chain price up 3.7% against prior 3.8%. In addition, Treasury's $22B auction of 30-year bonds tailed by 0.5 bp; bid-to-cover and foreign demand were both light of recent averages. Follows a tailing 10-year note auction yesterday. Friday brings March CPI, February factory orders and preliminary University of Michigan consumer sentiment and inflation expectations for April. Street looking for headline CPI +1.0% m/m in March and core CPI +0.3%. Headline spike not surprisingly driven by Iran conflict.

ï¿½                     Iran-US ceasefire hopes boosted by Israel-Lebanon negotiations: Some optimism around the Iran-US ceasefire emerged after Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu ordered his cabinet to begin direct negotiations with Lebanon; comes after Netanyahu spoke yesterday with Trump and envoy Steve Witkoff, who urged him to scale back Lebanon strikes and pursue negotiations (Axios, NYP). Favorable diplomatic development sparked a risk-on move in US equities after concerns over ceasefire durability intensified following fierce Israeli strikes on Lebanon Wednesday and Iran's warning it would abandon broader ceasefire if attacks continue (NBC, Reuters). Meanwhile, direct talks between US and Iran are scheduled for Saturday in Islamabad where VP Vance will lead the US delegation including Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner (Axios). Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains at an effective standstill; CEO of UAE state-run oil company said Thursday the strait remains closed and access restricted. Ship tracking data showed only one oil products tanker and five dry bulk carriers sailed through the strait on Wednesday (CNBC, Times of Israel). Additionally, there is growing focus on domestic political fallout of the war, with some commentators framing the conflict as a strategic US defeat and raising concerns about midterm impacts from war-linked affordability pressures (Politico).

ï¿½                     +8.5% STZ (Constellation Brands): FQ4 revenue and EPS beat with better beer shipments and margin the primary standouts, though FY27 beer net sales growth guidance lagged consensus; company also withdrew previously issued FY28 outlook amid market uncertainty.

ï¿½                     +5.6% AMZN (Amazon.com): CEO letter to shareholders talked up compute demand tailwind for AI (AWS's AI run rate over $15B in Q1) and momentum behind company's chips business; noted chip business annual run rate at ~$50B.

ï¿½                     +4.8% MRVL (Marvell Technology): Upgraded to overweight from equal weight at Barclays; said industry checks suggest optical ports should double in 2026 and double again in 2027.

EU Market Recap

UK (0.05%), Germany (1.14%), France (0.22%)

 

Synopsis

ï¿½                     European equity markets finish lower, but off worst levels. Follows Wednesday's big gains. Energy, utilities, insurance and financials the best performers, while luxury, autos, travel and tech the main laggards. Defensive tilt chalked up to some concerns surrounding ceasefire durability, though tensions in the region seem to have eased on Thursday and talks between US and Iran set for Saturday morning in Pakistan. Positioning dynamics still seen as supportive of an extended bounce. However, dampened rate reprieve a high-profile area of scrutiny. Structural damage already done and a limited Strait of Hormuz reopening some of the other overhangs. Market pricing in approximately two ECB rate hikes and one BoE hike by year end. Fed Funds futures saw implied probability of a rate cut this year go from 65% immediately after ceasefire announcement down to ~25% though markets leaned towards a hike before truce. On the European macro data front, latest update from RICS showed notable weakening in UK housing market. German industrial production missed and trade data saw rebound in exports. Spanish industrial production also unexpectedly dropped. In the US, Weekly initial jobless claims printed at 219K, above 210K consensus and prior week's upwardly revised 203K (was 202K). Continuing claims came in at 1794K against 1840K forecasts, below prior 1832K (revised down from 1841K); lowest since June 2024. February personal income down 0.1% m/m vs expectations for a 0.3% rise; personal spending up 0.5% m/m, in line with consensus. February core PCE at 0.4%, in line with forecasts and level with January. Final read of Q4 GDP down to a 0.5% SAAR vs 0.7% in the first revision; final Q4 chain price up 3.7% against prior 3.8%.

 

Macro

ï¿½                     European bonds have given back approximately half of Wednesday's big gains, which came on US-Iran ceasefire announcement. German Bund ~10 bps off yesterday's low near 3% and benchmark Gilt also more than 10 bps higher near 4.80%. Some investors think market may have been too aggressive in amount of tightening priced in, which contributed to yesterday's big rally (Morningstar). Bond market reflecting uncertainty over durability of US-Iran ceasefire and lack of clarity over future of Strait of Hormuz. Renewed volatility in bonds tracking energy markets, with WTI crude up ~6.5% after drop of approximately 16% in prior session. Brent also ~3.5% higher after falling ~17% on Wednesday. There are thoughts that bond markets will struggle to return to pre-war levels given notion of rates remaining higher for longer (Reuters). Market has calibrated rate hike bets somewhat, but damage to energy infrastructure in Middle East suggests energy-related price pressure will take time to subside. Most analysts have tipped June for first ECB rate hike rather than April. BoE outlook may hinge on Governor Bailey, who pushed back against rate hike expectations last week.

ï¿½                     Brussels warns that Europe still anticipating a stagflationary shock despite the US-Iran ceasefire (FT). European Commission preparing to cut its growth forecast and revise up its inflation outlook to reflect macro changes since its last update. Prior to the conflict, it forecast growth at 1.4% in 2026 and 1.5% in 2027, with inflation slightly above the ECB's 2% target. Growth seen slowing by approximately 0.5%, while inflation could increase by more than 1% depending on the length of time it takes for energy prices to return to pre-war levels. Handelsblatt highlighted that it could take months for the energy market to recover due to damage to energy infrastructure and uncertainty over access through the Strait of Hormuz. Europe is anticipating shortages and will be competing with Asia for its energy needs. Against this backdrop, it might be difficult for ECB to look-through the energy-driven inflation shock, even if the US-Iran ceasefire holds. Note, some analysts this week warned of potential policy mistake if policymakers misread macro picture and tighten when demand is likely to remain weak.

ï¿½                     German economy may be heading for a contraction, with industrial production data weakening even before the Iran conflict started. February update saw a 0.3% m/m drop versus consensus for 0.6% gain and prior revised to 0.0% from 0.5% contraction. Leaving if flat on an annualized basis. Decline in February due to 1.2% fall in construction and drop in manufacture of computer, electronic and optical products by 3.9% and pharmaceutical production down by 4.4%. A 1.7% expansion in auto sector production an offsetting factor. Bloomberg highlighted recent growth downgrade by leading institutes to 0.6% this year, half the pace expected just a few months ago. There was some encouraging trade data, with a strong 3.6% m/m rebound in exports and imports rose by 4.7%. However, sell-side economists expect a first quarter contraction. Despite the promise of a strong fiscal impulse, Germany has so far struggled to turn increase in spending into positive momentum. While government investment a support for infrastructure and defense, energy-intensive sectors impacted by high oil and gas prices, which is weighing on the industrial recovery.

ï¿½                     The London Times reported that experts warning UK households likely to face at least six months of pain, even if the Iran ceasefire holds. Supply chain issues likely to keep food prices underpinned and cost of fuel will remain high. Highlighted fuel and shipping markets continue to price in risk of escalation. Former director of shipping company Maersk Jensen said there is likely to be hesitancy among ships going through Strait of Hormuz because of risk of ceasefire breaking down. Guardian cited Lloyd's List Intelligence editor-in-chief Meade, who said with Iran in control of Strait, there is no change in situation. Goldman Sachs forecast that Brent will average $100/bbl through 2026 if the Strait remains closed for another month, with risk to the upside (Bloomberg). Its base case is for flows to pick-up from the weekend. Food prices are predicted to rise by 9% by year-end, energy price cap forecast to increase 13.5% from July and petrol prices up 19% since conflict started. As a result, UK growth will remain weaker, and inflation forecast to top 3.5%.

Asian Markets

Japan (0.73%), Hong Kong (0.54%), China (0.72%)

 

Synopsis

ï¿½                     Asia equities ended mostly lower Thursday but closed well off their troughs and within a narrow range in a cautious session. Japan, Greater China, Singapore and Southeast Asia benchmarks all fell while Australia and Taiwan posted small gains following weak openings. India underperforming again as the rupee comes back under pressure and oil ticks higher. Another day of few regional developments to knock equities off their cautious trajectory. RBNZ Governor Breman said New Zealand's growth remains tied to developments in the middle east and said the bank will act decisively if prices increase. Thailand's central bank said it would keep interest rates stable for 'as long as possible' to support the economy despite forecasts for higher inflation. Japan's March consumer sentiment deteriorated by the most since the early months of the pandemic, and missed estimates. Mitsui OSK Lines (9104.JP) said it will monitor the Gulf ceasefire before allowing its ships to attempt a Hormuz transit. BYD (1211.HK) said it will open as many as 20 sales locations with partners in Canada this year as Ottawa seeks alternatives to US. Eastern Air Logistics (601156.CH) shareholder Legend Holdings (3396.HK) has sold its entire 11.3% stake in the company to China Logistics Group (0GCPYN-E). Hanwha Aerospace (012450.KS) has suspended plans to buy Poongsan's (103140.KS) defense division. Lotte Chemical (011170.KS) is to spin off its Daesan Plant unit, then pursue a merger with HD Hyundai Chemical to strengthen vertical integration. Shinhan Bank (Shinhan Financial Group, 055550.KS) is to sell $2.1B Samsung Electronics (005930.KS) shares in one of South Korea's biggest ever block trades.

 

Macro

ï¿½                     Japan's latest sentiment indicators weaker: Consumer confidence index was 33.3 in March, well below consensus 38.3 following 39.7 in the prior month. Sequential 6.4 pt drop was the largest since the Covid shock in Mar/Apr 2020. All components were notably weaker. Results were intuitively seen to be a reaction to the spike in fuel prices amid the war in Iran. Inflation expectations logged a sharp rebound in the proportion of respondents looking for higher prices over the next year to 91.9%. More than reverses a drop in February (unclear what caused this, possibly recent declines in gasoline and rice prices) and back near a record high of 93.7% in May-02. Follows sharp declines in the March Economy Watchers Survey sentiment indexes designed to gauge business activity closely tied to consumption. Current conditions index also fell at the fastest pace since the pandemic year, weakness more pronounced by a double-digit drop in the outlook index. Fuel price impacts predictably wide-reaching across consumption sectors (retail, dining, other services) as well as corporate manufacturers and nonmanufacturers. Results contrast with positive direction in the BOJ and Reuters Tankan surveys released earlier, though these were discounted to some extent with responses submitted in the first half of March, while the latest data captured the latter part of the month, particularly the Economy Watchers Survey for which the survey period is from the 25th through the end of each month. Recall that BOJ was leaning hawkish at the March policy meeting as available data had not reflected much of the Middle East impact and higher fuel prices in real time reinforced inflation risks.

ï¿½                     Exodus from India equities continues despite middle east ceasefire: Overseas investors sold India equities for 23rd consecutive session Tuesday, Bloomberg data showed, pulling net $17.8B from India in late February through to 7-April. Data showed six of these days saw net outflow of more than $1B with total YTD already near record $19B seen for whole of 2025. Wednesday saw more net selling by overseas investors despite market uptick, article said, citing provisional data. But Sensex and Nifty 50 benchmarks only modest underperformers vis-ï¿½-vis regional exchanges as $20.6B of domestic institutional funds bought local stocks to offset exodus. Outflow added to pressure on rupee, which reached record low 94.6 per dollar before several RBI measures in past two weeks. India benchmarks under pressure even before Iran war erupted with its large-cap software services stocks underperforming from concerns over AI exposure. Investors concerned impact higher crude prices will have on public accounts, inflation and RBI response.

ï¿½                     China weighs financial support for its biggest airlines hit by rising fuel costs:  Bloomberg citing people with knowledge reported Chinese authorities are exploring financial relief measures for its state-run airlines as they are grappling with soaring fuel costs since war in Middle East broke out. Options included government subsidies, preferential tax treatment and state-backed low-interest loans, in what could be biggest support for the sector since Covid, although all the deliberations still at preliminary stage. Sources added that mergers are also being considered. Recall China's top three carriers, China Southern Airlines (1055.HK), China Eastern Airlines (670.HK) and Air China (753.HK) have already struggled to return to profit since Covid. The three cumulatively lost CNY209B ($30B) from 2020 to 2025. Soaring fuel prices recently piling more pressure on the sector as lack of hedging by Chinese airlines makes them more vulnerable to spikes in price volatility. Meanwhile analysts said the airlines may get some reprieve should the fragile ceasefire between Iran and US hold and more ships could traverse through Strait of Hormuz.
