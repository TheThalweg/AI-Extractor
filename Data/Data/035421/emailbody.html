Key takeaways
  • SPOT keeps adding positive, meaningful, and interactive engagement: more video, algo control, mixing tools, and IRL moments.
  • The TME read-across from Soda is misdirected and driven by uniquely Chinese dynamics. AI music is more complementary than competitive for distributors.
  • The mid-May investor day (first in 4 years) offers a forum to show how the company is shipping product updates and iterating in an AI world.
  • YouTube Premium/Music price increases are constructive for industry structure after wholesale step-ups and follow SPOTï¿½s mid-Jan price hike.
  • While modestly higher near-term opex growth leads us to trim our PT to $630 (~30x fwd FCF), shares offer >30% upside and an attractive risk/reward ï¿½ assume OW.

With this report, Sean Diffley assumes lead coverage of Spotify.

We are assuming coverage of Spotify with an Overweight rating and $630 PT (~30x ï¿½27 FCF/sh of ~$21), down from $650 (multiple unchanged), as we trim EBITDA/FCF by ~2% to reflect higher opex growth. We forecast a ~35% EBIT CAGR and ~20% FCF CAGR (fully-taxed) through ï¿½28. Our bullish view rests on confidence in Spotifyï¿½s ability to: (1) deliver low/mid-teens ex-FX revenue growth via product innovation and share gains (>40% share ex-China and set to cross 300mm paying users and 800mm MAUs this year on our forecast); (2) expand margins meaningfully (GMs in the mid-30%s in '27) with price hike tailwinds and mix shift to higher margin verticals; and (3) produce an above-consensus ~35% EBIT CAGR through 2028.

SPOT keeps adding positive, meaningful, and increasingly interactive ways for users to engage with the platform. Recent examples include more video content (~400mm users streamed video pods), Taste Profile (so users can control their algorithm), Mixing Tools (>50mm mixed playlists and >1mm transitions per day), and stronger lead-gen for concert ticket sales (>$1.5bn of ticket sales).

At its first investor day in four years, SPOT can give investors tangible examples of how it ships product improvements and iterates in an AI world, in a label-friendly way. We believe it remains more likely than not that Spotify launches its own AI content creation tools over time, in partnership with major labels. Given its 40%+ global subscription music share (ex-China), this move could quickly make it a leader and preferred solution.

No Regrets: Spotify notes that users regret <3% of time spent on the platform, versus >70% for many social platforms, while engagement per user is 3-4x higher than peers. This aligns with our Audio Entertainment survey, where Spotify leads in satisfaction (~90% very or somewhat satisfied), ahead of YouTube Premium/Music (~86%), Apple (~85%), and Amazon (~82%). Similar to Netflix, with >90% of revenue from subscriptions, total absolute hours matter less than they do for largely ad-supported platforms.

Increasingly Interactive: Interactivity positively correlates with price. For example, one hour of music streaming monetizes at ~$0.10 vs ~$50 per hour for live concerts, with video and gaming in between. Spotify continues to move from passive, individual listening to more interactive, dynamic, and increasingly social experiences.

What began as on-demand music now includes video podcasts (video is a ~10x larger market than audio), collaborative playlists (>50mm ï¿½mixed playlistsï¿½ and >1mm transitions per day), and artist tools such as tour alerts. While not financially material today, Spotify drives significant lead-gen for live events, helping facilitate >$1.5bn of concert tickets to date. Its rich data on user streams and preferences is also extremely valuable to artists and promoters.

New Genres & Possibilities: How consumer habits evolve with GenAI remains highly uncertain across media. However, we have a "glass half full" view that it will likely be accretive here, creating new music/video genres and increasing production velocity. Given accessible GenAI tools, output could increase sharply, and a bigger catalog is good for SPOT engagement. A Sonarworks survey of 1,000+ producers shows ~70% are experimenting with AI and 20% are regular users, primarily for efficiency in editing, mastering, and isolating audio.

We see AI music generators like Suno as more complementary than competitive, focused on creation rather than distribution. Spotify provides a scaled platform, a highly engaged user base, and the best monetization vehicle for new content. As a distributor and marketplace, Spotify is agnostic to shifting tastes and preferences, unlike IP owners.

While SPOT hasn't disclosed how much AI music is on the platform, smaller streaming service Deezer disclosed that nearly 40% of daily music delivered (or >60K tracks a day) are AI-tracks, but up to 85% of these streams are fraudulent and represent <1% of listening.

Why This TME Is Different: SPOT sold off ~5-10% following Tencent Musicï¿½s (covered by Yang Liu) recent slowdown, which appears driven by competition, including ByteDanceï¿½s Soda Music, amplified by TikTok/Douyin, as well as a strategic shift toward ad-supported tiers at the expense of ARPU. We don't believe a clear line should be drawn to Spotify, given that China and its music market are significantly different than Western markets across content ownership, copyright law, music consumption behavior, and distribution.

We see 5 key structural differences between China/TME and the West/Spotify:

  1. ByteDanceï¿½s cross-platform ecosystem creates a distribution flywheel not replicable outside China (TikTok Music shutdown outside China in 2024)
  2. Lower ARPUs and propensity to pay for premium subscriptions (paying subs to MAUs: ~22% TME vs ~40% Spotify) (Exhibit 9 and Exhibit 11)
  3. More localized and fragmented content preferences (>80% local in China vs >70% major-label driven in the West) (Exhibit 5)
  4. Weaker copyright enforcement in China enables widespread derivative content versus stronger IP protections in the West
  5. Spotifyï¿½s global leadership (>40% ex-China share) and latent pricing power (US pricing would be ~50% higher if it had tracked Netflix since 2018) (Exhibit 6)

Changes to our estimates: Our subscriber and revenue estimates over the next several years are broadly unchanged, with consolidated revenue up ~1% primarily driven by FX. Near-term we trim '26 Premium net adds by -1mm to +27mm (consistent with past two years), still above consensus, and re-weight net adds to be back-half weighted. This reflects both the benefits from product improvements to the free tier, but also the assumption those benefits take more time to flow through to paid net adds.

Our EBITDA estimates come down by ~1-2%, reflecting modestly higher Opex growth in '26, carried forward. We now estimate that underlying (adjusted for FX and social charges) operating expenses grow ~10% y/y in '26 - reflecting updated investment growth near-term. We continue to see that pace of growth moderating into the MSD-HSD% range. For 1Q we look for +3mm Premium net adds to 293mm and a gross margin of 32.8%. For 2Q we model +6mm net adds and 33.2% gross margin.
