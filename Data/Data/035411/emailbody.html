US equities were slightly lower Friday, S&P down 0.1% bringing to an end 7 days of consecutive gains. Over the week, S&P up 3.6%. US10yr yields up 4bps Friday to 4.32%, down 2bps over the week. Oil fell 0.8% Friday to USD95.2/bbl (brent) ahead of the weekend ceasefire negotiations.

US headline CPI in line, up 0.87%mom in March and 3.3%yoy. Core softer than expected, however, up 0.2%mom/2.6%yoy; consensus at 0.3%mom. The monthly change in headline inflation was the largest since June 2022; and was driven by a 21% surge in energy commodity prices. Still, Justin Weidner notes that the details lean softer for the core PCE deflator. Justinï¿½s nowcast for March core PCE is now at 0.22%mom, down from 0.27%mom previously. Overall: inflation in services ex-housing remains stubbornly elevated. At the same time, passthrough from tariffs and higher energy prices are clear upside risks going forward.

US Michigan consumer sentiment missed, down 5.7pts to 47.6 in April, lowest print on record. Both expectations and current conditions fell. One year ahead inflation expectations jumped 1ppt to 4.8% (history shows the series is highly sensitive to oil prices), 5-10yr ahead expectations up 0.2ppts to 3.4%.

China CPI missed, up 1.0%yoy in March, consensus at 1.1%yoy. But producer prices beat, up 0.5%yoy, consensus at 0.4%yoy. Yi Xiong notes that is the first positive print for producer prices in 41 months. Two forces have driven the turnaround: (1) the Iran shock that led to a surge in energy and commodity prices; and (2) the AI investment boom that has pushed up input prices for data centres. Yi now expects producer price inflation to rise to 3%yoy by Q4.

Following on from Matt Luzzettiï¿½s piece showing that on a range of model-based measures, Fed policy is currently accommodative, Matt Raskin notes that another way to judge the policy stance is by simply asking market participants where they think neutral is. NY Fed have been doing that for a number of years, and the change in estimates is striking: the modal expectation of nominal neutral has risen from 2-2.5% in 2022 to 3-3.5% today.

Michael Hsueh thinks goldï¿½s prospects have only been temporarily dented by the Iran conflict. Ultimately, gold should benefit from elevated betas to dollar and rates, and Michael thinks this is the likely direction of travel, leading to a USD 5,100/oz target for the second quarter. Still, in light of the Iran impact, he has moderated his year-end target to USD 5,800/oz (down from USD 6,000/oz), which is predicated on USD depreciation to -4% on the broad index. He still sees upside for silver towards USD 88/oz by year-end.

Debbie Jones notes that the outcome of negotiations held over the weekend will likely be key in determining future access through the Strait of Hormuz. As of Friday last week, tracking data pointed to minimal crossings. Crude exports from Saudi Arabia have been moving through Yanbu port and averaged a robust 4.5 mb/d last week. Attacks on Saudi Arabian energy facilities have reportedly resulted in a 600k bpd cut to production capacity, while throughput on the East-West Pipeline has been reduced by 700k bpd.
