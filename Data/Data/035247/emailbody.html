Global Markets Daily: The Case for Structurally Higher US Equity Volatility
9 April 2026 | 8:42AM EDT | Research | Economics

嚙踝蕭

We have focused recently on the potential for longer-dated equity volatility to move higher over time, even if equity prices continue to rise.

嚙踝蕭

Our models of the macro and market drivers of equity volatility suggest that longer-dated implied volatility "should" have been rising in recent years, driven largely by rising equity market concentration and a slowly rising unemployment rate.

嚙踝蕭

We think the concentration of value in AI-related areas of the market adds to the risk of higher volatility over time as focus increases on whether the benefits justify that value. Rising market concentration and high valuations嚙碼"non-macro" market forces that factor into our models嚙碼are not direct measures of those AI-related risks, but they are symptoms of the AI-driven market, as they were in the late 1990s tech boom, and so they are proxies for the impact that AI has had on equity markets in recent years.

嚙踝蕭

The recent rise in equity volatility is due to the Iran War, so we expect volatility to fall in the short term if the market reduces weight on downside macro outcomes. But there has been a structural rise in longer-dated implied volatility in recent months already, in excess of its usual relationship to the equity index.

嚙踝蕭

We see more than one route to higher equity volatility. The obvious one is a deterioration in the macro cycle, but we also think there is an underlying supportive force for longer-dated equity volatility. So we continue to see merit in pairing upside in longer-dated equity volatility with long risk exposures, especially on any relaxation in the near term, as we have seen recently.
 
Our macro models suggest that longer-dated volatility belongs around recent (higher) levels...
