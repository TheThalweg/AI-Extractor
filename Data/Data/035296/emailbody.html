Geopolitical spillover has shifted the mining debate from scarcity and consolidation to cost inflation, supply-chain fragility and demand risk. Sector valuations imply a slowdown, not a recession. We remain selective, favouring low-cost assets, resilient FCF and exposure to tighter commodity markets.

Resetting the debate. The sector debate has moved decisively away from copper scarcity and industry consolidation towards geopolitical spillover. Rising energy prices are feeding through cost curves, while disruption in fuel, sulphuric acid and freight is exposing the fragility of supply chains. New pinch points are emerging across the industry (see Owning the Bottlenecks) even as demand downside risks build. Aluminium sits at the centre of this shift: the combination of Middle East supply disruption and a rising/steepening cost curve is tightening fundamentals, with implications that will endure beyond any de-escalation. These disruptions will feed through more regionalised trade flows and higher premiums on local supply.

Cost pressures not inherently bearish. History shows that margins can still expand during inflationary periods, provided commodity markets remain tight. Our framework also shows that first-quartile assets are structurally less exposed to inflationary pressure than fourth-quartile mines. The key question is whether the current shock turns demand-destructive. Given the wide range of outcomes and heightened uncertainty, we prefer exposure to commodities that can better withstand a weaker macro backdrop, low-cost assets, resilient FCF, and disciplined capital allocation. That keeps us selective: we continue to favour Glencore, Norsk Hydro and Endeavour Mining, while copper equities with full valuations such as Antofagasta (Underweight) look more vulnerable, if the debate shifts further towards recession risk. Against that backdrop, we neutralise our industry view, reflecting a more balanced risk-reward as demand uncertainty increases.

A modest slowdown is priced in. Sector multiples have reset, but the de-rating still looks more consistent with a slowdown than with an outright recession or a balance-sheet stress event. Balance sheets and capital-allocation frameworks are materially stronger than in prior growth scares, which is why we view the sector as more resilient, and see a return to prior trough multiples as unlikely absent a deeper downturn. The relative P/B of SXPP versus the market stands at 0.78x, against a historical average of 0.83x, while absolute P/B is 1.65x, above its long-run average of 1.39x. The mark-to-market upside has largely faded for copper equities, but remains more pronounced for aluminium producers following the recent tightening in fundamentals. Steel is the notable exception to the old playbook: Europe is no longer operating in an open import regime, and CBAM, together with tighter safeguards, should strengthen millsï¿½ ability to pass through higher energy costs. ArcelorMittal remains our preferred way to express that view.
