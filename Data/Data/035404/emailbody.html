The Market Waits for No One

April 12, 2026

We enter the week with markets that still feel fragile to most investors but are likely further along in the discounting process than many appreciate. In fact, as discussed in our Weekly Warm-Up last Monday, the S&P 500 was in the process of carving out a low after hitting the low end of our targeted range of 6300-6500 for this correction.

Over the past several months, weï¿½ve consistently argued that this is simply a correction within a new bull market that began last April following the trough of the 2022ï¿½2025 rolling recession. The recent volatility and macro uncertainty doesn't change this underlying stance, given the persistent strength of the earnings recovery.

From our perspective, the correction began last October, with the S&P 500ï¿½s forward P/E declining 18% from its peak (Exhibit 1) ï¿½an outcome that typically coincides with a recession and/or an actively tightening Fed. We have neither in our base case. Beneath the surface, more than half of the stocks in the Russell 3000 have dropped 20% or more. To us, thatï¿½s not complacency but a market that has appropriately discounted the risks.

Importantly, earnings growth is moving the other direction and explains why the price damage has been contained to less than 10% for the S&P 500. That combinationï¿½falling multiples with improving earnings growthï¿½is a classic bull market correction, not a bear market.

This is also why the comparison to prior oil shocks is less concerning to us. In those cycles, earnings were already deteriorating or falling sharply. Today, they are accelerating from already high levels, and the magnitude of the oil move remains more modest by historical standards, particularly in real terms and in the context of sharply higher (10%+) tax refunds this year.

Other risks beyond the war in Iranï¿½private credit and AI disruptionï¿½also appear better understood by markets, with many stocks affected down 40%+. As our colleague Vishy Tirupattur argued in a recent Sunday Start, risks in private credit are material but not systemic. Private credit has tightened, but direct exposure for many banks remains limited and could ultimately drive share gains back toward traditional lenders.

On AI, the disruption narrative has moved faster than actual implementation. The enterprise application layer is still in early days, and in the near term, AI looks more likely to support, rather than compress, margins, particularly for early adopters. It also gives companies an excuse and an incentive to keep hiring low, as this new economic cycle continues to lead to operating leverage surprises on the upside. This positive operating leverage is surfacing in earnings growth and breadth as the median company is growing EPS in the double digitsï¿½the fastest pace since 2021 (Exhibit 2).

The equity-rates correlation has turned decisively negative again, meaning higher yields are once more a headwind for valuations. The recent hawkish pivot from central banksï¿½driven by inflation concerns tied to commoditiesï¿½is the primary source of the latest market anxiety and likely the final hurdle for equities to clear. Financial conditions are tightening, but ironically that dynamic is exactly what could allow the Fed to override its primary mandates. The question is whether financial conditions need to tighten further before they get easier. Part of the rally last week coincided with falling bond volatility after Chair Powell signaled a more neutral position.

The final phase of a correction is rarely easy and could require another re-test for markets, particularly if rates or bond volatility push higher again. But this would take us back to our broadening thesis, which was playing out before the Iran conflict. Therefore, we continue to favor leaning into a barbell of cyclicals where earnings remain strong and valuations have compressed (Financials, Industrials, Consumer Discretionary Goods), offset by quality growth where sentiment and valuations have also reset to healthy levels (the hyperscalers).

Further de-risking in crowded areas and intermittent volatility would be consistent with a market continuing to carve out a lowï¿½not entering a new bear market. However, markets arenï¿½t typically so gracious as to offer multiple opportunities, which is why we have encouraged investors to be early.

The bottom line is that markets are doing what they typically doï¿½discounting the future ahead of the headlines. We think much of the adjustment for geopolitical risk, private credit concerns, and AI disruption has already taken place. What remains is largely about rates and policy, which we believe will be resolved as the transition in Fed leadership is completed.

As always, the market trades in advance of the headlines. Investors should do the same.

Enjoy your Sunday.

What We Are Watching This Week

MONDAY, APRIL 13

Japan Money Stock: We forecast money stock M2 increases +1.7% YoY in March.

US Existing home sales: We expect existing home sales declined 2.2% m/m to 4.00m, which would leave sales 0.5% below where they were a year earlier. Pending home sales rose, on net, over the prior 2 months, but we think existing home sales had outrun this signal in February and expect some payback. Price appreciation has slowed significantly, but that has yet to result in a pickup in demand. Inventory data for condos/co-ops have been unreliable, initially showing large declines that are then revised away for that month but repeated in the following month. The National Association of Realtors has sped up its reporting of home sales, but the shorter survey period appears to have caused a mismeasurement of front-month inventories.

TUESDAY, APRIL 14

Singapore MAS Meeting: We expect the Monetary Authority of Singapore to tighten policy by allowing for a steeper pace of appreciation for the S$NEER policy band.

China trade: Export growth likely eased to 4%Y (vs. 21.8% in Jan-Feb). Import growth could remain elevated at 12%Y (vs. 19.8% in Jan-Feb).

US NFIB small business optimism: The index rose sharply around the time of the last Presidential election to about its average during the first Trump presidency. It then reversed about ï¿½ of the rise around Liberation Day, but since May 2025 has been trending sideways a bit below the average level during Trump 1.0. Most of the swing has reflected economic expectations. Other series have generally continued on trend. However, there is about a six-month uptrend in sales expectations. Labor market indicators (e.g., plans to raise employment, current job openings) are healthy but below pre-pandemic highs.

US PPI: Our preliminary estimate for March core PCE prices is a 0.23% m/m increase, with headline boosted to +0.62% by higher energy prices. The CPI report due April 8 will likely have an effect. Passenger airfare tends to be sensitive to energy prices, and they pose a risk of boosting March inflation. The source data for airfare in PCE prices is the PPI, not the CPI; so this PPI report will be an important indicator of pressure from higher energy prices on core services in PCE. Financial services PCE, which also relies on PPI inputs, is likely to be soft in March and April. We expect portfolio management and advice PPI to soften in those months, reflecting weak equity returns in February. By contrast, health services PPI (physicians and hospitals) is likely to firm after the downside surprise in February.

WEDNESDAY, APRIL 15

Japan Core Machinery Orders: We forecast core machinery orders decreases by -1.1% MoM in February.

EA industrial production: We foresee a decline of euro area industrial production in February, at ï¿½0.3%M after ï¿½1.5%M in January

US Import prices: Import prices will also contribute to the PCE price estimate, and potentially reflect an impulse to international airfares from higher oil prices.

THURSDAY, APRIL 16

China growth datapack: 1Q GDP growth likely picked up to 4.9%Y (vs. 4.5% in 4Q25). Industrial production growth may ease to 5.5%Y in Mar (vs. 6.3% in Jan-Feb). YTD growth of fixed asset investment could edge down 10bps to 1.7%Y in Mar. Nominal retail sales growth likely slowed to 1.8%Y (vs. 2.8% in Jan-Feb).

US Jobless claims: Initial claims rose 16k to 219k last week. We think this is a temporary swing around the Easter holiday rather than a sign of underlying weakening, and we forecast a decline to 215k in the coming week. If we're wrong, and new claims increase, the two weeks of higher prints could begin to suggest that oil prices and uncertainty were spilling into faster layoffs. We expect continuing claims revert to recent levels, rising to 1.83 million.

US Philadelphia Fed manufacturing: This is one of the first of the monthly manufacturing surveys that have much impact on our ISM estimate. Last month, it did not signal much change in the trend in manufacturing output growth despite the oil shock. Nor did the ISM measure, whose survey covered more of the month of March and covers more of the country. Despite last month's rise in oil prices, the prices paid index rose only slightly and remained below its highs after Liberation Day. It did, however, interrupt a three-month decline. Prices received similarly showed little new pressure but did not continue to decline.

US Industrial production: We forecast a 0.1% m/m rise in industrial production, led by nonauto manufacturing output.

UK Monthly GDP: Our nowcasting models are consistent with 3M/3M growth at 0.2%. Out bottom-up modelling suggests February growth at 0.1%M, with modest upside risks to this estimate. Following the March PMI data, our nowcasting plummeted to 0%Q growth for 1Q26. We do think the sentiment hit is overstating the scale of activity slowdown in March, and keep our 1Q growth forecast at 0.2%, with 2Q growth estimate at 0.1%Q. From here on out, more broadly, we expect flat activity, with downside risks over 2H.
