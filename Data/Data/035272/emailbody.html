Can the energy price shock push the UK into recession? 		     
 
 
01 April 2026
    Sanjay Raja
+44 20 754-50530
Send email 		      
 
 
      View report      
  (5 Pages)

The global energy price shock has raised serious concerns about inflation. The corresponding growth impact, however, has been largely ignored. With the UK facing its fifth supply shock in just around a decade, however, concerns that energy price rises could push the UK into a downturn have gained momentum.

On our current expectations, conditioned on energy curves from 18 March, GDP growth is expected to drop to 0.7% ï¿½ 0.35pp lower than our pre-conflict forecast. Tepid growth from late last year will likely stay as higher energy prices squeeze real disposable incomes, dampen investment/hiring, and add to general uncertainty. Given the starting point, the prospect of non-linear shifts in growth may be stronger ï¿½ particularly following a near 1pp increase in the unemployment rate last year.

In this note, we do two things. We look at past energy shocks and how they have impacted growth using a Hamilton-based energy shock measure. We then look at the prospect of non-linear shifts in the growth outlook adopting a probability-based model.

Our analysis shows the existence of non-linear effects on growth. To be sure, while we acknowledge serious upside inflation risks to the BoE's outlook, there are also serious downside risks to the BoE's growth outlook. The slowdown in GDP growth combined with a ~1pp year-over-year jump in the jobless rate mean risks are no longer solely one-sided. While the bar for growth to prompt a policy response is likely higher post-2022, policy-makers will need to closely monitor survey data for signs of deteriorating growth.
