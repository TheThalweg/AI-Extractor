Our belief that China will enter reflation 2026 has transitioned from expectation to reality.

PPI inflation turned positive for the first time in 41 months, rising to 0.5% YoY from -0.9% in February, while the MoM increase of 1.0% represented the largest gain in 48 months. Two powerful forces drove this turnaround: (1) the Iran shock that led to a surge in energy and commodity prices; and (2) the AI investment boom that pushed up input prices for data centers, such as optical fiber, data-storage units and electronic materials.

Looking at PPI components, two things are worth highlighting. First, although upstream prices rose the most, downstream prices also recently turned positive YoY, suggesting price pass-through to end-consumers is starting to take place. Second, the modest increase in overall energy/fuel input prices (3.6% MoM and -3.8% YoY) verified our view of China's resilience to oil-price shocks. This could further support China's export competitiveness this year.

CPI inflation moderated seasonally but remains resilient. Headline CPI eased to 1.0% YoY in March from 1.3% in February, reflecting the post-LNY drop in travel and entertainment prices and further declines in pork prices, but was offset by an 11% increase in gasoline prices. Core inflation held steady at 1.1% YoY, staying above 1% for 6 months (after adjusting for LNY seasonality), albeit its upward momentum has moderated somewhat, in part owing to the drop in gold prices.

We revise up our 2026 PPI inflation forecast to 1.8% (+1.0ppt), expecting that it will reach 3% by Q4. Our 2026 CPI inflation forecast remains unchanged at 1.5%.
